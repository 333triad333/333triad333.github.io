Early Human History

The Paleolithic Age saw humans living in small hunter-gatherer bands. Sustenance was secured through foraging for fruit and the use of fire and simple stone or wood tools. During the Neolithic Revolution, humans learned to domesticate animals, improved stone tools, invented agriculture and began to live in sedentary settlements, giving up the nomadic life of the Paleolithic Age. The Neolithic Age was succeeded by the Bronze Age, where humans made tools of copper and tin. A gendered division of labor emerged, with men hunting and women foraging.

The Development of Civilization

Civilizations require the development of agricultural surplus to produce and sustain their conditions of life. Key accomplishments that assisted in the fulfillment of this necessity include the transition from residing in villages to living in cities and the invention of irrigation. Early civilizations emerged, historically, in river valleys, locations able to facilitate and sustain population growth. Western civilization arose in the fertile crescent, southeast of Anatolia and encompassing the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. Monarchy was the most common political system in early Western civilization. 

 
The Fertile Crescent

Mesopotamia

Cities first emerged in the Tigris-Euphrates valley, a region known as Mesopotamia. Early Mesopotamian religion was polytheistic, with many gods serving different functions. Fortune in human affairs was thought to be a result of pleasing or displeasing the gods. Myths such as The Epic of Gilgamesh sought to explain the human relationship to the natural environment and the will of the gods. Later civilizations would inherit Mesopotamian myth and religion in the further development of their own traditions.

Writing was invented by the Sumerians to keep track of accounting using a system of symbols called cuneiform. Eventually, writing was used to produce literature and record information, such as mathematics and foreign languages.

People of Mesopotamia

Sumer

Sumer, in the south of Mesopotamia, was composed of small warring cities who fought over control of the water supply. Victors consolidated their territory, leading to increasingly larger settlements, unification being desirable for efficient use of irrigation.

Uruk and the Emergence of the City

The first city to emerge in world history was the city of Uruk, in southern Mesopotamia. In the late fourth millennium, the beginning of the Uruk period shows the development of settlements of unprecedented size.The largest of these was Uruk, in the southern Babylonian region. The cause of the increase in the settled population is uncertain, but historians postulate a number of scenarios, including population growth encouraged by agricultural development, immigration due to climate change, and perhaps an unpredecended settlement of semi-nomadic populations.

Uruk functioned as a city center surrounded by secondary settlements, including towns and villages. With a new way of living came changes in the structure of human society, including the emergence of a social hierarchy and economic specialization. Social life revolved around temple complexes, which took vast amounts of human labor to construct and maintain. These temples, dedicated to various gods, provided the people of Uruk with a sense of purpose and community.

The economy was centered around agricultural production supported by a complex irrigation system. Ecological variety encouraged specialization of labor. While most people were involved in agricultural production, city life encouraged a small number to specialize as artisans. Specialized production created a need for exchange; exchange required centralized organization, which in turn necessitated a shared ideology that would appeal to the people of Uruk and ensure their participation in this system of exchange. This ideology centered around the gods and the temple system, where people would make offerings to the gods; the temple was the site of the collection and redistribution of goods. The temple system led to the rise of administration and a bureaucratic class. The requirements of temple administration necessitated a system of accounting, which would lead to the development of writing.

Labor specialization led to the development of social hierarchy, with prestige attached to one's role. Most people were engaged in agricultural production and their lives centered around the family structure, while others held a role in the temple bureaucracy, which was stratified. At the top of the social hierarchy was the "priest-king" in charge of the temple. Dependent laborers survived on rations in exchange for their services.

The writing system in Uruk is referred to as proto-cuneiform, as the characteristic wedge found in cuneiform proper was not yet in use. Proto-cuneiform was not just used to write in the Sumerian language, but also Akkadian and various other Semitic tongues. In order for accounting to function, a system of weights and measures was needed beyond the proto-cuneiform script. This early system laid the foundation for all subsequent Mesopotamian systems of accounting. The basic units of this system were ordered by a combination of sexagesimal and decimal systems. A year was twelve months long with thirty days to each month.

The middle of the fourth millenium saw the Uruk expansion, where the culture of Uruk spread and came to expand its sphere of influence outside of the south of Mesopotamia, in partiuclar western Iran, northern Syria and the south of Turkey. Susa, a city in Iran, came to reflect the influence of Uruk in the late Uruk period, as attested by the presence of tablets and Uruk pottery excavated in the area. Susa came to exert influence over a vast area in Iran. In the north, the influence of Uruk was present but less penetrating. The Uruk expansion caused widespread change in social forms across the Near East, though it is unclear how much of the tendency towards urbanization was organic, merely accelerated by exposure to the southern culture. Most scholars attribute the cause of the Uruk expansion to elites seeking foreign resources.

At the end of the Uruk period, local cultures returned to indigenous traditions and, in the north, village life again became the norm. Susa became a state comparable to Uruk; this formation is known as the proto-Elamite state. The proto-Elamites developed their own script used across Iran for around two hundred years.

The Early Dynastic Period

During the Early Dynastic period, Uruk's influence across the Near East had waned; people returned to indigenous traditions and ceased the use of writing. However, in Babylonia, written production increased dramatically. After the passing of several centuries, Babylonia re-established contact with outside civilizations.

During the Early Dynastic period, the predominant form of social organization was the city-state. Settlements existed near rivers, as irrigation was required to support agricultural production. The most important city-state during this time was Lagash, which incorporated the cities of Girsu, Lagash and Nina. In the third millenium, Babylonia saw an increase in population and the number of cities. Each city featured a hierarchy under a central leader. While initially separated by swaths of steppe and lands not used for agriculture, expansion due to increasing population meant that their territories began to overlap. This eradication of buffer zones between city-states led to important changes, including centralization and secularization of power.

Mesopotamian ideology saw each city as the home of a particular god or goddess, overseeing distribution of food to the people. The head of temple administration had the most power in Mesopotamian society. When city-states' zone of influence increased, wars over agricultural land led to the increasing importance of military roles in leadership, rather than cultic functions. Historians see this as evidence of a "primitive democracy" that developed into a dynastic system over time. This basis for authrity was incompatible with the previous valuation of the cultic role. The new military class is associated with the emergence of the palace. Lagash demonstrates the harmonization of these two sources of power and authority, where God and king were seen as cooperating despite distinct functions. However, the final independent ruler of Lagash, Uru'inimgina, collapsed this distinction: transferring ownership of land and estates to the city-god and his family, he ruled by divine right, erasing any distinction between secular and sacred authority. This idea would be seen in its greatest expression during the Akkadian period.

The competition over land is most clearly seen in the 150-year war between the neighboring states of Umma and Lagash. Kings portrayed themselves as fighting wars on behalf of the gods; the conflict was seen as a war between Lagash's patron deity Ningirsu and Umma's Shara. Lagash claimed that the chief god Enlil had demarcated the border in ancient times, a mythical claim to their land. These wars over territory were long and frequently inconclusive.

However, not all relations were hostile, and dimplomatic relations between neighboring states existed. Interactions between states, both hostile and not, led to increasing centralization of power. Conquest and unification of powers, in addition to political alliances between friendly states, encouraged centralization. A mutual commitment to a shared ideology and religious system existed between city-states. At one point, the focus of the cult shifted to Nippur and cities were united under a common pantheon headed by Enlil, Nippur's patron deity.

The Babylonians traded with partners outside of the south, in particular with the Gulf region. Access to Omani copper mines were important due to the new bronze technology. In addition, the Babylonians traded with the Elamites, importing luxury goods. In the area of northern Iraq and Syria, a new material culture was developing, though the area would not see urbanization until around 2600. In the north, agriculture rlied on rainfall rather than irrigation, and consequently, cities tended to be smaller in size. Secular authority was more dominant in the north. Like the south, the north was a multilingual society. Political organization in the north was essentially the same as in thr south.

In the Early Dynastic period, society was organized into "households," including families and their servants. The household was hierarchically organized, with male and female workers at the bottom. These workers were rewarded with rations for their labor. Most workers were engaged in repetitive manual labor.

Writing evolved significantly during this period. Instead of tracing, scribes used a reed to inscribe the characteristic cuneiform wedge into the clay tablets. Signs became more schematic and standardized, moving away from pictorial representation. Signs increasingly came to render syllables, reflecting spoken language to a greater degree. Writing came to acquire greater functions, preserving knowledge and conveying information to an increasing degree.

Akkadians

The Akkadians were Semitic-speaking semi-nomads originating in the deserts west of Mesopotamia. Their king, Sargon, conquered Sumer after settling in the central area of the Tigris-Euphrates valley, unifying the territory under an Empire that came to span not only Mesopotamia, but beyond into the Iranian plateau and Lebanon.

In the Late Third Millenium, the north of Babylonia saw centralization under the Akkadian kings, ending the previous political organization comprised of city-states. Rising to prominence in Kish, Sargon relocated the center of rule to the city of Akkad. Akkad became known for its military power. Sargon's primary rival was Lugalzagesi, who ruled over several southern cities, including Uruk and Umma. Akkadian rule required a new form of social organization, and Sargon attempted to subordinate the city-states to himself. This was not entirely successful, and rebellions were frequent. Still, the court enacted centralizing policies, including a new system of taxation and a standard system of weights and measures.

In addition to adapting to new accounting techniques, Akkadian scribes had to adjust to the Akkadian language. Cuneiform script was adapted to this purpose, which required increased use of syllabic signs. However, the Sumerian language remained in use in non-official settings.

The Akkadian leadership confiscated estates from city-state officials for their own use. In addition, the Akkadian dynasty attempted to achieve ideological unity through connecting traditional gods to the royalty. Akkadian leaders established a tradition of giving daughters control over the priesthood of Nanna at Ur, for example, giving her access to considerable wealth.

The Akkadians shared power with other kings, but none were as successful. The Akkadian kings focused their military exploits across western Iran and northern Syria, encountering states such as Elam, Parahshum and Simurrum. In even more remote regions, the Akkadians procured rare goods such as silver and hard stone, which were sent back to Babylonia. Governance of conquered territory varied based on local needs and circumstance; for example, in Susa, use of the Akkadian language was enforced in bureaucratic settings, though regional leaders retained their Sumerian titles.

How much influence the Akkadian Empire retained in the provinces is a matter of question. Though documents can be found written in Akkadian over a wide range of territory, this does not necessarily indicate anything more than the spread of scribal practices. In distant areas, Akkad maintained control through diplomacy, including marriages.

The extensive power of Akkadian kings influenced their self-perception; taking part in extremes of self-glorification, Naram-Sin, for example, created a new title that meant "king of the four quarters of the universe." Militarily successful, he proclaimed himself a god. A cult of a living king was an unprecedented development in the Ancient Near East. This change was reflected in the arts, which produced an exceptionally refined sculptural style.

Akkadian power proved to be unstable, probably due to its many enemies outside of the state. The Akkadian kings saw many rebellions, the most elaborate account occurring under the reign of Naram-Sin. Occurring between the northern king Iphur-Kish, the southern king Amar-girid and Naram-Sin, it seems that centralized Akkadian power was widely despised. Naram-Sin won the day, and it was probably following this battle that he declared himself a god.

Outside of Babylon, Akkad faced further opposition, in particular from the Iranian state Marhashi, to the east of Susa. Marhashi, with Elam, fought a notable battle with the Akkadians at the confluence of the Diyala and Tigris. The most severe threat, however, came from the eastern mountain people, the Guitans. The Guitans, during the time of Sharkalisharri, began to settle in Akkad in large numbers, leading to conflict. This combination of internal and external threats led to the unravelling of the Akkadian Empire during Sharkalisharri's reign. The Near East again reverted to the rule of independent states. Gutians acquired several city-states and often referred to themselves as the heirs to the Akkadians, despite sharing rule with other states, including Lagash.

With the decline of Akkad, several new states emerged outside of Babylonia. These included Urkesh and Nawar in northern Syria, Mari and Awan, with Susa as its capital. A new king, Puzur-Inshushinak, emerged in Akkad and unified southwest Iran, occupied portions of northern Babylonia, and the Diyala valley. He became the target of the military ambitions of Lagash and the first ruler of Ur III, Ur-Namma, who destroyed his state.

The legacy of the Akkadian Empire impacted the entire Near East, never having seen such extensive military campagins or rulers like Sargon and Naram-Sin.

Third Dynasty of Ur

The Sumerians came to resent Akkadian power and eventually regained rule of Mesopotamia through a dynasty centered in the city of Ur. This lasted for around 100 years, followed by a period of chaos between warring dynasties where no single dynasty claimed total control. By the next period of reunification, the Sumerians no longer retained their group identity.

Fragmentation after Akkadian rule did not last long; 50 years post-Sharkalisharri, king Utu-hegal, succeeded by Ur-Namma, inaugurated a new dynasty centered in Ur. Utu-hegal expelled the Gutians from Babylonia; Ur-Namma, continuing his work, forced autonomous city-states into submission; following this, he claimed the title "King of Sumer and Akkad," meaning the whole of Babylonia. Under Ur-Namma, Susa was captured from Puzur-Inshushinak of Awan. Thus began the Third Dynasty of Ur. A series of five rules reigned for around 70 years. The state of Ur III did not leave the same lasting impression on Mesopotamia as the Akkadians did; while later Mesopotamian records continue to recount the legends of the Akkadian kings, Ur III did not inspire similar long-ranging memorialization, and subsequent discussion of Ur III focused on its failures in the context of justifying new dynastic power. Ur III left an incredible amount of historical documentation that is nearly unparalleled in ancient history.

Ur III was smaller, but more centralized. The heart of Ur lay in Babylonia, with outlying areas of control to the East. Ur III was built primarily during the reign of King Shulgi. Ur was divided into about 20 provinces corresponding to the previous independent city-states. Power often passed from father to son, and kings used marriage to secure his position. The governers controlled the temple estates and maintained the canal system. In addition, a military administration run by generals existed in parallel. The administration developed a system of taxation in the provinces, with collection tailored to the province.

Sumer and Akkad were espeically prosperous under the Ur III dynasty. Urbanization reached a high degree, and elaborate architectural constructions reflect this prosperity. Huge quantities of labor were necessary and many workers were employed by the state. Service was compensated with rations; as state bureaucracy was not totalizing in its power, adequate compensation for labor was required to attract workers.

This sophisticated state organization applied at home, but the provinces were subject to a different sort of administration: the military administration was responsible for governance, enacting a system of direct exploitation. The generals were required to exact a specific amount of tribute, paid with cattle, sheep and goats. Beyond the provinces, hostile regions were the site of military campaigns. Competing powers were appeased with diplomatic means, including marriages. This was ultimately unsuccessful, as many states remained hostile, including Shimaski, which would play a crucial role in the demise of Ur. These outlying areas primarily provided mineral resources. The Akkadian hostility towards Syrian states were replaced with diplomatic relations during the time of Ur III.

While Ur III had a smaller territorial reach than the Akkadian Empire, it was more centralized: state intervention in the economy was extensive, and the writing system was standardized across its territories. In addition, the kings attempted to unify the state ideologically. King Shulgi was treated as a deity, and represented all of Ur's territory, rather than being a local deity over a particular city-state. However, this centralization was not total, and local hierarchies survived. This is evident in the fact that after the decline of Ur, city-states were able to continue functioning, rather than collaping as they would if a part of a totally centralized system.

The cause of the decline of Ur is uncertain, but both internal and external factors are likely responsible. The final blow, however, came from Shimashki. Ibbi-Sin, the last king of Ur, lost control of Susa and Elam, who later came for Babylonia itself. The ruler of Shimaski captured Ibbi-Sin and Elamites occupied Ur for seven years. Eventually, they were chased out by Ishbi-Erra, who claimed the kingship; however, he did not hold the same authority as previous kings. Independent city-states again emerged in Babylonia.

Amorites

In the early second millenium, the Near East was politically fragmented and dominated by constant conflict between competing kings. Brief periods of unification occurred, but dissolved quickly. Cuneiform writing became common across the Near East.

The Amorites were the next people to unify Mesopotamia. Also known as the Old Babylonian dynasty, they ruled for around 300 years. The Amorite king Hammurabi wrote the most extensive law code dating from Mesopotamia, outlining a rigidly stratified, hierarchical social structure. Slavery, though often temporary, was a major feature of Amorite society.

The term "Amorite" was used to refer to groups of semi-nomadic pastoralists common to the Near East. These semi-nomadic pastoralists lived in settled villages along the river valleys in the summer and inhabited the steppes in the winter. Their livelihood revolved around renewable resources that livestock gave: sheep's wool, skin and horns. Nomads and sedentary people's activities complemented each other. The nomadic peoples provided animal products, while sedentary peoples provided agricultural and craft goods; farmers often provided fields for the nomadic peoples' flocks to graze on, which encouraged fertilization and plant growth. Because of this arrangement, city-states were capable of exercising some power over the nomads, who were subject to taxation and military and labor levies. Semi-nomadic pastoralists followed a system of tribal organization, and people claimed descent from a common ancestor. Despite this, tribal affiliations were loose, and people sometimes changed tribes. Nomadic groups that had villages near sedentary settlements were subject to the most control by settled peoples. Nomadic groups that settled further out were harder to control, and frequently came into violent conflict with farmers over resources.

Sedentarization of pastoral nomads tended to occur among the wealthiest and poorest of the group. Wealthy pastoralists' herds grew to the point of unmanageability, causing investment in land and settlement; poorer nomads could not support themselves on their few animals, so settled to try and secure employment, including in the army. Around the turn of the third millenium, records show increasing numbers of people designated as Amorites in the censes of city-states across the Near East. Amorites were subject to negative portrayal, and were blamed for the collapse of Ur III. Following the disappearance of the Ur III state,some emerging dynasties claimed Amorite origin, in contrast to their negative depiction in the literature. This included King Hammurabi of Babylon.

After the disappearance of the Ur III dynasty, the Near East did not immediately collapse into fragmentation, though decentralizing tendencies were strong and the region saw a number of competing and quickly succeeding kingships. Most important were the dynasties centered at Isin and Larsa. Independent states arose in the east, and Elam remained outside of Babylonian control. Competition escalated into unceasing warfare. Despite this, Babylonia still understood itself as united under a common religious system centered at Nippur, and control over that city allowed one the title "King of Sumer and Akkad."

The first successor to the state at Ur was the Isin dynasty, recognized by the priesthood at Nippur. Following a brief period of peace, a rival dynasty arose at Larsa, and the king attacked the Isin king, challenging his leadership. After a period of internal instability, the Kudur-Mabuk dynasty acceded the throne. The Kudur-Mubuk king Rin-Sin was the longest reigning king in Mesopotamia; Rin-Sin captured vast swaths of territory, extending his family's control, already ranging from Nippur to Larsa and Ur, to include Uruk. After Rim-Sin's defeat of Isin, only Babylonia was left to oppose him; Hammurabi, an Amorite king, would soon take the throne, defeating Rim-Sin in his old age.

Despite the decentralizing tendencies present during this period, Babylonia did not see economic decline. However, in the early second millenium, the Babylonian economy would see a partial privatizatiion, where state laborers would work part-time for others and institutions started to contract out for work and services. Instead of paying dependent laborers in year-round rations, when labor was needed free laborers would be hired and paid a salary in exchange for, for example, farming or a set amount of wool delivered. The long-term effect of this system was catastrophic for Babylonia; many people could not meet high demands for contributions and so asked for credit and emergency loans at high interest. This placed an enormous strain on debtors. Kings attempted to combat the debt crisis by annulling consumer loans, and it was the palace that endured the loss.

This situation of decentralization and warring kings in Babylonia reflected the situation across the Near East.

Hammurabi

Hammurabi inherited a state that, over time, had come to encompass a collection of previously independent northern cities. At the time of his accession, Rim-Sin had just unified the south of Babylonia. The rise of Hammurabi begins the Old Babylonian period, which lasted around 1500 years. Hammurabi was drawn into conflicts during this chaotic time, but most of his focus seemed to be directed toward internal affairs in Babylonia, including digging canals and fortifying cities. He was incredibly successful in his military campagins, and was a talented diplomat. He used his diplomatic ability to win allies and betrayed them when he was able to do so. It only took Hammurabi five years to cement his rule over southern Mesopotamia. He was successful in defeating Elam, Larsa, Eshnunna and Mari in a very short time. He was less successful in his campaigns in the north, nevertheless, he was the strongest king at this time.

Hammurabi had exceptional attention to detail, which allowed him to fulfill his duty as the "shepherd" and "farmer" that kings were expected to be at this time. He is most well-known for his law code, which was written primarily to glorify himself and the justice of his kingship. The law code reveals the tripartite structure of the time's social hierarchy. Punishments enshrined in the law code took social standing into account.

By the end of Hammurabi's reign, the Mesopotamian political landscape had completely changed. Babylonia was the single great power in the region. However, shortly after his death, his son faced rebellions and gradually lost control of the southern portion of the state. However, northern Babylonia continued flourishing under the rule of five successors. Northern Babylonia had a flourishing literary culture during this time, and students underwent complex and elaborate training in mathematics, music and other subjects. Scribal training may have been intense in response to the Sumerian language being threatened. This period saw the production of the first text of The Epic of Gilgamesh, along with other important Mesopotamian texts. This period saw the first extended written mathematical documentation, including measuring fields, harvests, numbers of items and calculating volumes, among many other uses. The mathematical knowledge of this period is highly sophisticated and demonstrates an understanding of algebraic logic. The Babylonians had correctly calculated an approximation to the square root of 2, and were aware of the underlying facts concerning the Pyhagorean theorem.

Babylonian political dominance led to religious supremacy: the patron deity of Babylon, Marduk, was integrated into the Sumerian pantheon at Nippur by becoming the son of Ea, who was already the god of Eridu. A few centuries later, Marduk would become the primary cult of Babylonia. The Old Babylonian dynasty set many of the main cultural elements of subsequent Near Eastern history. With the decline of the south of Babylonia, the north became culturally prominent.

Hittites, Kassites

The Old Babylonian empire disintegrated after being assaulted by the Hittites, from the north in Anatolia, and the Kassites, from the East in present-day Iran. The Kassites establied rule for 300 years.

After a short period of centralization, the Old Hittite Kingdom emerged in Anatolia. The earlier history of Anatolia is shrouded in mystery, but what is known seems to suggest that there was a network of small kingdoms, many speaking Indo-European languages.

A ruler named Hattusili founded the Hittite state in the early to mid-seventeenth century. He was heir to Kussara and swiftly defeated his central Anatolian competition. Capturing Hattusa, he made this central Anatolian city his capital. The city was vulnerable to attacks from the Black Sea, in particular from a group known as the Kaskeans. Hattusili expanded southward, perhaps to secure access to cereal fields. The kingdom of Yamkhad was subject to repeated Hittite invasions, but managed to remain independent. By the end of his reign, Hattusili had established a large state. On his deathbed, he appointed his grandson as his heir, who destroyed Aleppo and Babylon. With Aleppo sacked, other entities rose to fill the vacuum. Almost nothing is known about the sack of Babylon. Again, Mesopotamia and Syria fell to decentralization and fragmentation and provided no benefit to the Hittites.

Following the destruction of Babylon by the Hittites, the Near East entered a dark age. Urbanism was at its lowest point and many cities were abandoned. The lack of centralized power left a gap in the hisotrical record. The most important events of this time were the rise of the Kassites in the south and the Hurrians in the north. A Kassite dynasty arose in Babylon, and the Hurrians established a state in the north. Despite their dominance, their culture left almost no lasting impact in the Near East. The Hurrians, however, may have invented the horse and chariot. Seafaring may also have developed during this time.

Assyrians and Eastern States

The Assyrians followed a system of privatized, long-distance trade, establishing themselves as middlemen exchanging wood and metals. Mesopotamia had been dominated by a centralized state monopoly until the introduction of a market-based system by the Assyrians.

The Assyrians, located in their capital of Assur, conducted long distance trade that was particularly dependent on the situation in Anatolia. The Assyrians imported tin and textiles, tin being required for the production of bronze. Trade was conducted by riding donkeys through the Taurus mountains to Kanesh, where merchandise was exchanged for gold and silver that was brought back to Assur. The goods were then distributed through a series of smaller trade networks in Anatolia. Profit margins were very high, with tin doubling and textiles tripling their value.

East of Assur, the most powerful states were Eshnunna and Elam. Elam was the most involved in Mesopotamia at any point in its history during this period. Eshnunna became powerful after achieving independence from Ur during the reign of Ibbi-Sin, expanding control over the Diyala valley and capturing cities close to Assur.

When Zimri-Lim acceded the throne at Mari, Eshunna sought an alliance to bring an end to formerly hostile relations between the states. This resulted in a war where Mari pledged submission to Eshunnah. Soon after, Elam formed an alliance with Mari, Babylon and likely, Larsa, pillaging Eshunnah and installing a puppet ruler. However, an Eshunnian king replaced him and helped Hammurabi to destroy Elam; Hammurabi then betrayed Eshunnah and sacked it.

While heavily involved in the events at the time, Elam's exact role remains unclear. After Elam attained independence from Ur, king Ibbi-Sin was taken captive until Isin's armies liberated the city. Elam thus had little subsequent influence in Babylonia. After Eshunnah disappeared as a prominent political actor, Elam separated itself from Mesopotamian affairs, despite its undeniable importance.

Egypt

Egypt was the first centralized society, ruled by a monarchy. The Egptians possessed their own pictographic script, known as hieroglyphic. 

 
The Old, Middle and New Kingdoms

The Unification of Egypt and the Old Kingdom

Around 3050 BCE, Menes, also known as Narmer, unified the Nile valley. By approximately 2687 BCE, this dynasty ruled over a large, centralized state known as the Old Kingdom. The Great Sphinx dates, according to most scholars, to the time of the Old Kingdom.

Surrounded by vast deserts running the length of the Nile on both the Eastern and Western sides, Egypt enjoyed protection from foreign invasion, unlike the city-states of Mesopotamia, which suffered frequent invasion. The Nile flooded predictably, unlike the capricious flooding of the Tigris and Euphrates, much to the Egyptians' advantage. Agriculture was the most valuable economic possession.

The First Dynasty inaugurated a system of statewide taxation, aiding significantly in centralization. This system of taxation allowed for increased production. A system of government that divided the Nile Valley and delta into provinces was developed and the buereaucracy expanded, as demonstrated by the increase in royal tombs found dating from this period. Around 2750, the last king of the First Dynasty died, succeeded by Hetepsekhemwy. Hetepsekhemwy recentered the location for royal burials at Saqqara instead of Abdju.

During the Second Dynasty, Egypt saw internal tension and turmoil. Lower Egypt rebelled against centralized control. Upper Egypt, centered around the cult of Seth rather than Horus, produced king Peribsen. The last ruler of the Second Dynasty, Khasekhem, paved the way for later Egyptian aspiration. Khasekhem erected large monuments in an outward display of authority. This was backed by his effort at Egyptian reunification under his power. This reunification again brought prosperity, both culturally and economically, to Egypt.

The Third Dynasty saw the construction of the first pyramid, the Step Pyramid, under Netjerikhet. The Step Pyramid can be attributed to the great Imhotep, one of Netjerikhet's most important officials. The successors of Netjerikhet never reached the same stature as their predecessor.

During the Fourth Dynasty, three pyramids were built, the Great Pyramid of King Khufu being the most notable. This feat was exceptionally difficult and required massive amounts of backbreaking labor on the part of Egypt's lower classes. Khufu's predecessor, Sneferu, defined a new relationship between the pharoahs and the population: the king's word established ultimate truth, the king being divine. Sneferu built the Meidum pyramid, grander than the Step Pyramid, to project his power. Following this, he embarked on the failed project of the Dahshur pyramid, which proved architecturally unsound. Khufu surpassed his father's ambitions with the building of the Great Pyramid at Giza, a project of astounding proportions that required highly coordinated and long-lasting labor over two decades. Khufu's succesor, Djedefra, inaugurated the cult of the sun god, Ra. The pyramids, along with the increased power of the monarchy attendant to the cult of Ra, mark the important features of the Fourth Dynasty.

The Fourth Dynasty wished to project an outward image of stability while covering up conflict within the royal family. The later rulers of the Old Kingdom's attempts to resolve these crises led to a redefinition of Egyptian monarchy. The Fifth Dynasty, staring with King Userkaf, presented itself as heading a new age for Egypt. Userkaf returned to the pyramid as his choice of tomb, against Shepshekaf's departure. This new tomb was located near the Step Pyramid, a symbolic gesture. Userkaf, rather than emphasizing his absolute power, focused on the sacred character of his role and his relationship to Ra. Pyramid building had weakened Egypt economically and rulers could not afford to continue erecting giant monuments; kings needed a new way to project power. This was done, again, by emphasizing the divinity of the king's office. Userkaf opened up government service to non-royal families, creating a class of professional bureaucrats.

The Fifth and Sixth dynasties saw elaborate, ornate archiectural developments, with civil servants using construction as a means of competition. King Unas, leaving no successor, was followed by the commoner Teti. Teti needed his administration to protect his tenuous claims of legitimacy. Teti was assassinated by a member of the royal family. Teti was succeeded by Pepi. Soon, Nubia wished to reassert its independence, becoming a serious threat to the Egyptian state. Egypt lost control over Nubia. Along with decentralization in the hands of local officials gaining increasing power, low flooding of the Nile, and the long reign of Pepi II, the Old Kingdom saw its decline.

Egyptians of the Old Kingdom believed in an afterlife for the royalty, who were divinely appointed to generate maat, a force that brought order to the kingdom. Like Mesopotamian religion, Egyptian religion was polytheistic and good fortune was secured through acting rightly. The pyramids were giant tombs erected as a monument to the rulers' piety and divine status; these structures housed mummified bodies that were believed to preserve them for the afterlife.

The Old Kingdom was highly stratified, though men and women enjoyed a more egalitarian relationship than the surrounding societies.

The Middle and New Kingdoms

The Old Kingdom began to weaken around 2200 BCE, the pharoahs unable to remain convincing in their power. This led to civil wars and decentralization of rule in the hands of nobles. This time, lasting until around 2060 BCE, was known as the First Intermediate Period.

Centralization was restored in the period of the Middle Kingdom. This stability lasted until the invasion of a Semitic-speaking people known as the Hyksos, beginning the Second Intermediate Period. Hyksos rulers introduced elements of culture from other Near Eastern states, including war chariots, increasing Egypt's military strength.

The Middle Kingdom saw the flourishing of Egyptian literature, though it is lacking in the impressive architecture of the Old Kingdom.

After the death of Pepi II, Egypt was subject to a succession of weak rulers who ruled for short periods of time. Egypt fragmented, power being in the hands of regional leaders, the nomarchs. But the ideology of kingship persisted in the Egyptian mind: a suzerain, a 'first among equals,' would come to reign over lesser kings. This terrible king was Kheti. Kheti, however, was not universally recognized as king. After a century, Kheti's inability to feed his subjects led to his downfall. The low Nile floods led to widespread hunger, and men who could provide could use this to their power. One such king was Ankhtifi, who gained control over three southern provinces; these would shortly come under the control of Thebes. Intef of Thebes would engage in a conflict with the rival Gebtu province, making extensive use of Nubian fighters. Intef's successor would conquer Gebtu, defeating the house of Kheti.

The Egyptian civil war between the house of Kheti and the Thebians would last for over a century. Thebes continued to expand, and under Intef II, defeated their opposition. Thus, Theban power extended over the southern provinces. Intef III was succeeded by Mentuhotep II, who aspired to unify all of Egypt beyond the southern provinces. Subduing the rebellious Tawer, Mentuhotep II marched northward and took Herakelopolis itself. Thus began the Middle Kingdom.

The decay of the Egyptian monarchy in the First Intermediate Period made life more uncertain. This situation led to an increased focus on the afterlife. The Egyptians would invent original sin, a conception of the underworld, the final judgment and the promise of resurrection, all which were to influence Christianity. In the Old Kingdom, the afterlife was reserved for the king. During the First Intermediate Period, access to the afterlife expanded to include a greater number of people. This began in the time of Pepi II, when his sister was afforded a small pyramid with inscriptions normally reserved for the king. The cult of Osiris became prominent during this time, becoming the universal god of the dead and assimilating features of various other gods. The revered burial site at Abdju became the center for Osiris worship.

The Twelfth Dynasty of the Middle Kingdom pursued a model of absolute rule that went beyond what the Old Kingdom saw. Coming to power in a bloody coup d'etat, the king set about restoring the power of the monarchy. Amenemhat I inaugurated the building of massive temples, a change from the pyramids, which served as tombs for kings. King Amenemhat positioned himself as the bringer of an Egyptian renaissance. He endeavored to secure Egyptian borders, constructing fortified bases along the northeastern frontier. In the south, Nubia remained a threat; Amenemhat carried out extensive surveillance before launching an assaut on Wawat, conquering the territory. Amenemhat was assassinated and succeeded by his son, Senusret I, who publicized the murder in order to control through fear created in crisis. During the Twelfth Kingdom, literature flourished, largely being comprised of propaganda supporting the regime. During this period, Egypt was active in trade and had an expansive military presence; Egypt became more multicultural as a result of these factors. The most notable king of the Twelfth Dynasty, Senusret III, saw the height of preceding developments. Senusret neutered the power of the nomarchs and established a system of fortresses.

During the unstable Thirteenth Dynasty, Egypt saw a succession of 50 kings in 150 years. Fortresses turned to other powers for funding, leading to Egypt losing control over its borders. A man named Nehesy came to fill the power vacuum. A weakened Egypt was conquered by the Hyksos.

The Second Intermediate period came to an end when the Thebans of southern Egypt defeated the Hyksos and reunited the kingdom, ushering in the New Kingdom. During this period, the pharoahs expanded the reach of Egyptian territory into the Levant and Anatolia. Reaching the eastern Mediterranean shoreline, the Egyptians came into contact with the Hittites, beginning a series of wars.

Under the Hyksos, the capital at Hutwaret was transformed into a town of Asiatic culture, worshipping the Canaanite god Baal. A small dynasty at Thebes managed to retain some power. The retreat of the Hyksos at Thebes allowed the Thebans to attempt to revive the glory of the Middle Kingdom, leading to a renaissance.

King Kamose of Thebes reclaimed Wawat from Kush and then marched on the Hyksos at Hutwaret. Dying prematurely, a successor would need to continue his work of reclaiming Egypt from its occupiers. This successor would be King Ahmose, who brought the Thebans to victory against the Hyksos. Ahmose knew that Egypt would need access to gold and so set out on the reconquest of Nubia. Ahmose, having secured the borders and the supply of gold, set out to make the royal family an object of devotion.

Unable to have children, Amenhotep designated his lieutenant Thutmose to succeed him. Following on the suppression by Ahmose, Thutmose declared war on Nubia. Next, determined to eliminate the Kushite threat, he attacked Kerma. Thutmose was intent on expanding Egypt's borders. Sensing threat from Mittani, Thutmose struck preemptively and successfully. When Thutmose I died, the Kushites revolted. His successor, Thutmose II, died prematurely, leaving the kingship to his wife, Hatshepsut, to rule as regent. Her reign is known for extensive architectural construction. She was succeeded by Thutmose III. His expansion of Egyptian territory turned Egypt into a multicultural state.

The early pharaohs of the New Kingdom promoted one god, Amun-Re, above other gods, which Akhenaten would take further with the sole worship of his sun god, Aten. Only the ruling monarchs had direct access to Aten. The cult of Aten prefigured the development of monotheism later on.

First Civilizations

Three centers of civilization emerged from 2200 BCE to 1000 BCE: that of the Hittites in Anatolia, the Minoans in Crete, and the Myceneans on the Greek mainland.

The Hittites

Like the Egyptians, the Hittites developed a unified, centralized state with authoritarian rule. The Hittites, from the Caucasus in the north, spoke an Indo-European language. They worshipped both Indo-European deities and gods inherited from the conquered Anatolian populations. A warlike people, the Hittites came into conflict with the pharoahs of the New Kingdom and various peoples south of Anatolia in their aggressive, expansionist wars of conquest. The conflict with Egypt led to a a standstill that cuminated in the signing of the first international peace treaty.

The Minoans

The Minioans organized their society into city-states, unlike the Hittites. The Minoans are referred to as such after the Greek myth of King Minos, who is said to have locked his son, a monster called the Minotaur, in a labyrinth of his own construction, as well as commanding the first great navy. The Minoans commanded a powerful navy used to maintain their great maritime empire, financed through trade with the Hittites, Egyptians, and the dominated Aegean peoples. This revenue was used to furnish their civilization with grand and elaborate palaces. The Minoans are also known for the development of Mediterranean polyculture: the simultaneous cultivation of olives, grapes and grains, which greatly contributed to their flourishing.

The Mycenaeans

Like the Minoans, the Myceneans organized their society into city-states. Known as the first Greeks, the Myceneans spoke an Indo-European language and inhabited the Greek mainland. The Myceneans were a warrior culture consumed by conflicts between settlements, which tended to be clustered on the coast due to infertile land and the predominance of useful ports. Closely connected to Minoan civilization, they eventually came to dominate Crete, a fact known by the presence of tablets written in an early Greek script known as Linear B on the island. By the time of Mycenean dominance of Crete, their civilization was constantly at war not only on the mainland, but abroad as well. The epic poem The Iliad may depict historical events from this time.

The Regional System and the Late Bronze Age Collapse

From 1500 to 1200, a regional system emerged that united Near Eastern states, in addition to states outside its borders. A few large states dominated with smaller polities depending upon these larger states. The great powers included Kassite Babylonia, Hittite Anatolia, Egypt, Elam, Mycenae and the Mittani state, which was succeeded by Assyria. The centralized nature of these great states left an abundance of documentation.

During this time, the great powers simultaneously saw a cycle of creation, flourishing and then, decline. The emergence of the regional system came after the preceding Dark Age, which saw fragmentation and a paucity of historical documentation. With the re-emergence of centralized polities, the new states of the Late Bronze Age were unprecedented in their size and cohesion. These new polities went beyond dynastic powers controlling a number of city-states: they operated as countries, becoming regional powers. The autonomous city-state had disappeared, even though the ideology of the city-state remained. The only exception to this tendency was in the Syro-Palestinian world, where the basic organization of small, autonomous city-states remained, including Jerusalem and Ugarit, among others. This area served as a buffer zone between competing great powers, with the Syro-Palestinian region being subject to vassalage at the hands of great powers.

During this time, each state was aware of its position in the political hierarchy, and frequent back and forth communications between them were the norm. Babylonian was the language of diplomacy and communication occurred in this language. The best evidence for this comes from the Amarna Letters, the clay tablets recording communication between the Egyptian kings Amenhotep III and Akhenaten and kings of other powers. The Amarna Letters reveal a hierarchy with kings of equal standing and lower-ranked vassals. The primary interests of the kings, as revealed in the Amarna Letters, was exchange of messengers, goods and women. The letters of vassals to overlords reveal different priorities. These letters discuss political situations in their polities and contain accusations of other vassals' unfaithfulness to the kings. This was done to avoid payment of taxes and tribute by shifting the focus to other vassals. The kings engaged in proxy warfare through vassals, seeking to gain greater power through conquest of other kings' vassals. Even between equals, the Amarna Letters show much infighting and bickering. Relationships needed to be codified in law, and this was done through treaties between rulers. These treaties took two forms: treaties between equal powers, and treaties between kings and vassals. Treaties between equal kings were probably drawn after conflict or during other special situations. Treaties between a king and a vassal outlined the vassal's responsibilities to the king, including providing taxes and military contingents. The Hittite vassals were not allowed to engage in war with other vassals nor could they contact other great kings. Exclusive loyalty of the vassal to the great king was expected. Further, allegience was secured through marriages, which were very important to the history of these states. These marriages underscore the familial relations of equal kings. Another frequent topic in the Amarna Letters is the exchange of gifts, which further increased cohesion between the great powers. Frequent complaining about the stinginess of other kings is present in these letters. This gift exchange among elites was enabled by an extensive trade network throughout the Near East, with certain locations, such as the vassal state Ugarit, acting as trade hubs.

Despite diplomatic relations between great kings, warfare was a prominent feature of this era, with great powers competing for territory. Wars were fought by picking off vassals or through direct confrontation. In areas lacking large buffer zones, combat tended to be more direct. One of the most famous direct conflicts was the war at Qadesh between Egypt and the Hittite state. Warfare was used to adjust power relations rather than cause outright destruction of enemies. As such, warfare co-existed with the diplomatic relations between states. Warfare in this period had changed from earlier times with the development of new technology, such as horse-drawn chariots. Warriors formed a military elite that sae political prominence.

The Near Eastern states had a sense of all belonging to one social organization, producing a shared ideological conformity. The elite class had more in common with one another than in lower classes. This was also a time of great cultural production, with most of Egypt's well-known tombs and temples being constructed during this era. Public buildings were commonly located in clearly dmearcated and protected areas of cities. The cities were named after kings, reflecting their purpose of serving royalty rather than common people. They also attest to conflict between rulers and the kings' desire for prestige. A strict social hierarchy existed between palace dependents and free villagers; the palace dependents, while not free, enjoyed greater wealth than villagers. The palace organization saw the greatest stratification, with serfs on the bottom, specialist artisans and craftsman above, and military elites at the top. Free people were still subject to government control, being required to pay taxes or provide labor. Despite this, they owned their fields communally, and received some support from the palace. Many of the poor fled the states, being unable to escape from indebted servitude, and formed groups in inaccessible mountains or deserts.

Mittani

The Mittani state was centered in northern Syria and, at its greatest extent, spanned from the east of the Tigris to the southern coast of Anatolia. An absence of records leaves much of Mittani's history shrouded in mystery. Around 1500, there was a ruler with regional powers, probably enabled by the Hittite desruction of Aleppo. This state was comprised of several vassal areas subject to the king. Mittinian dominance lasted until the mid-fourteenth century, and was Egypt's major opposition, competing over vassal states on the Syrian coast. The Mittani state became divided during the period of 1365 to 1335 due to competition within the royal family who sought outside support for their claims to the throne, leading to the western half of the state under Hittite control and the eastern under Assyria. The Assyrians took advantage of the weakening of Hittite power under the rule of Adad-narri I and Shalmanser I to turn Mittani into a vassal state. Mittani resisted Assyrian control throughout the rest of its history, but was unsuccessful. The last Assyrian king, Tukulti-Ninurta I, reigned around 1200, and the decline of Mittani centralization probably had to do with the events of this period.

The Hittite New Kingdom

The kingdom of Hatti was located in central Anatolia. Its expansion was directed southward, and at one point controlled western Syria. The life of the Hittite state was short, and had two periods of strength, called the Old Kingdom and the New Kingdom. The state's competitors were Egypt and Mittani, as well as some of the vassal states. Suppliluliuma I was the founder of the New Kingdom, reversing previous territorial losses. This king made Western Mittani his vassal and expanded territorially to Damascus. However, his reign ended disastrously when he Suppiluliuma died of plague. His succcessors could not consolidate his gains.

The battle of Qadesh with Egypt crowned several centuries of rivalry over Syria, and ended with Hittite victory and a peace treaty between Egypt and Hatti, inaugurating a defensive alliance. This alliance was likely provoked by the threat of Assyria in the region. However, a succession conflict led to Hittite decline and the fall of the state under Suppliluliuma II.

Most of Hittite influence was achieved not directly, but through vassal states in Anatolia and Syria that sometimes switched loyalties. The Hittites did not attempt to impose a unified culture or ideology and left local traditions intact.

Syria-Palestine

The Syria-Palestine region remained fragmented and subject to vassalage, particularly as ground for proxy wars between Hatti and Egypt. Egyptian rule was more direct than Syria-Palestine under Mittanian influence. Egypt focused on collecting tribute, ignoring local problems. The Hittites, after the subjugation Mittani, challenged Egyptian influence in the area after acquiring the Mittanian vassal states. This arrangement remained stable until the time of Sety I and Ramesses II of Egypt, when Egypt attempted to expand further north in the region. Muwatalli sabotaged this at the battle of Qadesh.

The vassal states of Syria-Palestine were completely dependent on foreign powers, frequently switching alleigence at the reversals of their masters' fortunes. The scribes of the Syro-Palestine region left many important documents, uncluding the Ugaritic Baal Cycle.

Kassites

After the Hittite sack of Babylonia, a power vacuum emerged, leaving an opening for the establishment of a new dynasty. The Kassites, a people who were relative newcomers to the area, spoke their own language and were seen as separate from the native population. While successfully establishing political rule, the Kassite culture did not leave much of an impact on the Near East. However, Kassite social organization persisted after their period of rule. During the Kassite period, the idea of the Babylonian state as such took hold. A Kassite king again unified Babylon, and the state was legitimated as one of the great powers of the Near East by its neighbors. The Kassites invested in architectural projects. The end of the Kassite dynasty came at the hands of Assyria and Elam. The Assyrian ruler Tukulti-Ninurta I installed a puppet dynasty in Babylon during a period of expansion; after a rebellion and Elamite pressure, the Kassites were briefly returned to the throne. Following Elamite raids on Babylon eventually ended the Kassite dynasty in 1155. Kassite control was followed by the short-lived second Isin dynasty. Kassite rule was eseential for the development of an Akkadian literary culture. A literary dialect known as Standard Babylonian developed during this time, and this dialect remained predominant throughout the rest of Mesopotamian history. Marduk increased in importance during this time. The recovery of an important statue of Marduk by King Nebuchadzzar I likely inspired the writing of Enuma Elish, reflecting the ideological centrality of Babylon during this time. Babylonian influence on Assyrian literary production began at this time.

Assyria

The Middle Assyrian period saw the development of Assyria from a settlement around Assur to a sizeable territorial power. This history is usually elaborated as a series of kings. The first notable king in this series was Assur-uballit I, who established control over the Assyrian heartland, establishing himself as someone of international significance. A diplomatic letter sent to Egypt prompted the Babylonian king Burnaburiash II to claim Assyria's status as a vassal, but Assyria's importance was unquestionable. A series of three kings, Adad-nirari I, Shalmaneser I and Tukultu-Ninurta I transformed Assyria into a major power. They focused on the west, turning most of Mittani into a relationship of vassalage. Acceptance of Assyria into the rank of the great states was gradual, but could not forever be denied. By 1050, Assyria's size was restricted to its heartland after continuous assault by Arameans. Assyria remained obscure for a century.

Middle Assyria was very much a military state, and Middle Assyrian society reflected this.

Elam

While Elam entered into the relationship of great states, it had secondary importance. After Hammurabi defeated Elam, it likely lost its unity. This situation was reversed around 1400. The Elamite state was large and culturally diverse. Evidence during this period is sparse. The Middle Elamite state was formed from the second dynasty of this period, founded by Igi-halki. The last rulers of this dynasty attacked Babylonia, which was at the time under control of Assyrian puppet rulers. After the Kassites regained control, the final king of this second dynasty, Kidin-Hutran, died. The king Shutruk-Nahhunte of the third dynasty carried out many invasions of Babylonia, bringing back loot, including the stele of Naram-Sin and Hammurabi's law code. This king eventually put an end to Kassite rule, but did not take Babylon. Elam became the strongest power in the region after the vacuum left by the Kassites. The Babylonian resurgence under Nebuchadnezzar I recovered the statue of Marduk and put an end to Elamite power.

The Collapse of the Regional System

Political violence and, possibly, climate change substantially weakened every extant state in the region by 1300 BCE. Various seafaring groups known as the Sea Peoples invaded and assaulted civilizations in the region. The Hitties' trade routes were cut off and, likewise, the Egyptians' trade network was destroyed. During the time of the invasions of the Sea Peoples, the Myceneans were consumed with internal conflict, hastening the downfall of their society. During this Dark Age, new peoples settled in the Near East and iron replaced the use of bronze. Another important development was the domestication of the camel.

The Re-emergence of Western Civilization

Following the violence of the era spanning from 1200-1000 BCE, the Neo-Assyrian Empire emerged, inspiring the rise of other civilizations in turn.

The Western States: Arameans, Neo-Hittites, Phonecians

The western portion of the Near East remained fragmented. Political organization typically consisted of small city-states ruling outlying areas. After the fall of the Hittite state, the king Kuzi-Teshub filled the power vacuum in northern Syria; his state fragmented into a number of kingdoms known as the Neo-Hittite states. The Neo-Hittite states were wealthy, and their elaborate palaces inspired admiration in Assyria.Urartu and, later, Assyria, exercised increasing influence over the neo-Hittite kingdoms.

Phonecian states, too, continued into this period. Centered on the Levantine coast, Phoenecians were known for their luxury goods and aptitude as seafaring merchants. The Phonecians established trading colonies in Morocco and Spain, among other places. Phoenecians faced intense competiton with Greeks and Rome, starting with the establishment of Carthage. The spread of the alphabet is the most enduring Phonecian contribution.

The Arameans were a new population to the west, seizing control over states throughout northern Syria. Their primary opponent was Assyria, but when not at war with the Assyrians, the many Aramean states fought amongst themselves. The Arameans usurped power from the neo-Hittite states in the north of Syria, and Damascus in the south.

The western portion of the Near East was a multicultural area shaped by Assyrian power.

The Neo-Assyrian Empire

The Neo-Assyrians were conquerers known for their brutality. First capturing Babylon and then Egypt, the Neo-Assyrians came to rule all of the Near East, albeit only for a brief period. The empire's subjects came to resent the harsh rule of their leaders, and a series of rebellions ensued. One such revolt in the seventh-century significantly weakened the empire. The Neo-Assyrians were defeated by the combined forces of the Medes, an Iranian group, and the Chaldeans, a Semitic people.

Neo-Assyrian expansion occurred in two waves: an earlier period that led to reconsolidating its extent in the late second millenium, and a second period in which it became a military empire. A militaristic society, Assyria was structured around the army, and served the war god Assur. Under the leadership of the king was a hierarchy of generals, the highest-ranking of which controlled the provinces. At first, campaigns would take place in the summer, after the harvest, when men were available to fight. Later on, armies were available to fight at any time. The army fought with the use of terror, rarely extending themselves into battles; the army would approach enemy territory and, if their foes did not immediately surrender, the Assyrians would sack villages that proved easy targets. The army, after conquering an enemy, would brutalize inhabitants, making an example of them. These campaigns resulted in Assyrian dominance of huge swaths of the Near East. The Assyrians carried out extensive relocation of the subject populations in order to meet their need for manpower. One consequence of these deporations was a mixed population across the Near East, including the spread of Arameans and their language. The Assyrians were able to engage in massive construction projects due to their campaigns. Rulers such as Assurnasirpal II, Shalmaneser III and Sargon II built massive, elaborate palaces.

Assyria had weakened greatly in the twelfth to tenth centuries, though it maintained control of its heartland even through the Dark Age. In the last portion of the tenth century, Assyria began conducting military campaigns to rectify this situation. This included population resettlement in newly conquered areas. Palaces were built and canals developed. The kings Assurnasirpal II and Shalmaneser III laid the basis for Assyria's future, reconquering the second millenium extent of Assyria through military campaigns and development, particularly in the north of Syria. During this period, incorporation of Syria was not the goal, requiring only that these states paid tribute. The Syrian states remained independent. During this time, Urartu was a serious threat. In the time of Assurnasirpal II and Shalmaneser III, Assyria was not an expansionist state, maintaining a distinction between those states of "the land of Assur" and those "under the yoke of Assur," ie, those states subject to Assyrian governance and those that remained independent under vassalage.

After Shalmanaser III, the Assyrian state saw decline. Its high degree of centralization made it vulnerable to the impact of dynastic struggles, and states in Syria held back tribute. Local governers became largely independent and many claimed kingship.

Assyria in the mid-eighth century was in a state of disarray. A series of powerful kings emerged starting with Tiglath-pileser III, who campaigned to expand the borders of Assyria. Unlike during the earlier period of Neo-Assyrian strength, Tiglath-pileser III sought to incorporate areas into the Assyrian state rather than merely collecting tribute. Neo-Assyrian dominance occurred in three stages, only escalating from one form of rule to another only when the king could not secure obedience. First in this ladder was vassalage, where local rulers remained autonomous but paid tribute; second, the installment of puppet rulers who were loyal to Assyria; lastly, these outlying states were annexed as provinces directly under Assyrian control. The Assyrian state sought to create a buffer between themselves and their Egyptian and Arab competitors while creating a system to maximize supply of resources for its core.

The Neo-Assyrians were never able to fully control Babylon, and showed marked respect for its culture, much of which had shaped the culture of Assyria. Its other rivals, Elam, Egypt and Urartu, were not extended the same respectful treatment as Babylon. For a short time, the Assyrians also dominated Egypt. The longest-reigning Assyrian king, Assurbanipal, acceded the throne when his father Esarhaddon appointed him to rule in Assyria, and his brother the Babylonian kingship.

The Assyrian kingship was seen as divinely granted. The king's purpose to create order through conquest, an idea promoted to the people through victory parades and demonstrations. The entire population of Assyria was to be loyal to the king, and at times collective oaths were sworn. Assyria had an active literary culture, particularly under Assurbanipal, who curated an extensive library. Recordings of omens make up a significant portion of Assurbanipal's library, revealing their importance in Assyrian thinking and culture.

While facing internal conflict and succession crises, various peoples sought to revenge upon Assyrian power in retribution for the arrogance of the state and their mistreatment. The Medes, along with other peoples including the Scythians, sacked Ninevah. The Assyrians relied on support from Egypt, and managed to maintain power in portions of western Syria for another two years. Babylonia succeeded Assyria as the dominant power in the region.

The Medes were a lowland people who inhabited the Zagros mountains. Their proximity to the Khorasan road, central to trade, caused great anxiety among the Assyrian leaders. Wehn Assurbanipal defeated Elam in 646, the Medes were able to expand, filling the power vacuum. Under their leader Umakishtar the Medes successfully sacked Assyria, with the help of Babylonia.

The Neo-Babylonian Empire

Similar in extent to the Neo-Assyrian Empire with the exception of Egypt, the Chaldeans were known for their cultural achievements. The Chaldeans rebuilt Babylon on a much grander scale and made significant contributions to astronomy.

In the early first millenium, Babylon was weak and struggled to maintain its territorial independence. The Kassites had been replaced by the Chaldeans, with Chaldeans and Arameans controlling large areas in the countryside. Independent city-states provided support to kings and preserved the culture; kingd succeeded each other quickly and it appears that, at times, Babylon may not have had a king at all. Assyria conducted frequent military campaigns during this time.

Following the death of Assurbanipal and the weakening of Assyria, a Chaldean, Nabopolassar, ascended the Babylonian throne. Nabopolassar united all of Babylonia, and was instrumental in assisting the Medes to sack Assyria. Though Assyria had declined, Babylonia still had to face Egypt as its main competitor. Nebuchadnezzer II was the Neo-Babylonian empire's greatest military leader and oversaw the Babylonian exile of the Hebrews. Babylonia is best seen as the successor to the Assyrian empire and its splendor captured the imagination of the ancient world. Succeeding Nebuchadnezzar was Nabodnidus, a controversial figure who is depicted in many different ways across sources. His devotion to the moon god Sin caused conflict with the cult of Marduk. The height of this conflict occurred when he relocated the capital of Babylonia to Teima in the desert, perhaps a move anticipating Persian conquest of the capital. Cyrus captured Babylonia in 539, portraying himself as a defender of Marduk.

During this time, due to policies of deportation, Babylon inaugurated a multicultural empire. Urbanization was at a peak, and cities were the dominant form of settlement. The Babylonian leadership saw themselves as heirs to an ancient tradition and there was much focus on reviving older cultural practices.

Elam and the Persian Empire

During the early first millenium, Elam repeatedly allied with Babylonia against the Assyrians. The result of these skirmishes were often indeterminate. In 664, Elam atatcked Babylonia, ending a relatively peaceful period. Ten years later, the Assyrian king Assurbanipal invaded Elam and beheaded its king; later, Babylonia would enter a civil war with Assurbanipal, with Elam supporting Babylonia. Assurbanipal turned against Elam, ransacking Susa and deporting the population. Despite this, Elam continued, despite being significantly weakened and reduced in size. During this period, Elam was politically unstable, with families vying for power. Local lords had great power and wealth. Various states occupied the Anshan highlands, out of which the primary enemies of Assyria would develop.

One of the most important eastern states was Urartu, though little is known about it. Urartu presented a formidable challenge to the Assyrians. Unified by king Sarduri I, the Urartuans built networks of mountain fortresses that allowed them to control trade routes. Urartu saw its greatest power under king Sarduri II. Assyrian expansion led it into direct conflict with Urartu; the Assyrians proved unable to conquer the state and relations became centered on diplomacy. Urartu likely saw its demise at the hands of unknown northern peoples.

Cyrus the Great founded the Persian Empire in the area of modern-day Iran. Cyrus was a talented diplomat and extended a high degree of religious tolerance to his subjects. By positioning himself as a defender of the God Marduk, he won the support of the Babylonians against their native king, who worshipped a non-traditional deity. The state religion of Persia, Zorastrianism, espoused a monotheism that would have a significant impact on later religions. Another important feature of Zoroastrianism was its dualistic cosmology: the world is the result of a battle between two opposing forces, good and evil.

At its greatest extent, under the rule of Darius I, the Empire spanned from Thrace in the west to India in the east. 


The Persian Empire at its greatest extent under Darius I

The Persian state began when Cyrus defeated the Median king Ishtumegu, claiming all of his territory. Cyrus would march on Babylon and extend his rule over the entire extent of the neo-Babylonian Empire before turning his attention to the northeast in Iran and Central Asia. By the time of Cyrus' death, the Persian Empire was larger than any state previously known. His successor Cambyses extended the empire to include further teritory to the west and became king od Egypt. He conquered Libya, Thrace and western India.

Under Darius, the Ionians revolted against Persian rule. Darius responded by sacking Greek cities, arriving in the bay of Marathon, where he was defeated by the Athenians. Retalition came under Daruis' son Xerxes, who sent massive troops to demand Greek submission. Defeating the Spartans at Thermopylae, the Persians sacked the empty city of Athens, which had been evacuated with Greeks retreating inland. The Greeks intended to fight at sea, and destroyed the Persian navy at Salamis. Forcing battles to occur where Persia could not fully deploy its massive army was the key to Greek victory. The Persians never again attempted to conquer Greece.

Persians were innovative in their methods of rule. Covering such a large extent of land and peoples, the Persian kings used local myth and tradition to justify their power. Cyrus, for example, portrayed himself as a champion of the Babylonian god Marduk. The previous ruler, Nabonidus, was a devotee of an irregular cult, and Cyrus could position himself against him in this manner. Cambyses did the same in Egypt, positioning himself as a son of Ra and taking an Egyptian name. The same approach applied to bureaucratic forms: where functional, the Persians did not intervene.

The Persian aristocracy was ethnically uniform, even if peoples of other groups held lower positions of power. The king was devoted to one god, Ahuramazda, who would later come to figure in Zoroastrian religion.

Darius acceded the throne after Cambyses, whose brother declared himself king while Cambyses was in Egypt, following its conquest. Cambyses died on the way back to the center, and a member of the elite, Darius, murdered the brother. Darius had no hereditary right to the throne and came up with an elaborate story to justify his rule. He was able to trace his lineage back to the founding dynasty, though not Cyrus' line; instead of tracing his family back to Teispes, he claimed he was descended from Teispis' father, Achaemenes. Under the kingship of Darius, the structures of the Persian Empire were firmly established. Darius divided the Empire into administrative districts, each headed by a satrap. Persian rule was flexible, adapting its form to concrete circumstances in each satrapy, as the state incorporated a wide variety of lands and peoples.

From Xerxes until the time of Alexander, the empire was decadent and unstable.

The Israelites

The Israelites' contribution to Western Civilization was monotheistic religion, which would become the dominant form of religion in the West through the widespread adoption of Christianity among the gentiles. According to the Biblical account, the origin of the Israelite people can be traced back to Egypt at least, and possibly Ur. Abraham, the patriarch from which the Israelites are said to descend, left Ur for Canaan under the inspiration of the god Yahweh. Originally polytheistic, the Israelites came to worship one god above all others. Yahweh is a composite figure likely fusing Aten from the time of Hebrew residence in Egypt, a Sumerian mother goddess figure common to many pagan religions, among other, prior deities.

The Israelites left Canaan for Egypt following Jacob, Abraham's grandson, to escape famine. By the thirteenth century, the Israelites had been forced into slavery. The Biblical book of Exodus tells the story of Moses leading his people out of Egypt into the desert. While in the desert, Moses ascends Mount Sinai to form a covenant with Yahweh, who promises to lead the Israelites to a promised land in exchange for exclusive devotion and adherence to his commandments.

The Israelites then returned to Canaan. The Israelites were divided into twelve tribes, a formation that remained until the emergence of the monarchy in the eleventh century. Saul, the first king of the Israelites, was succeeded by David and Solomon. The Israelites built up a large amount of wealth, which Solomon used to build the extravagant Jerusalem temple. When Solomon died, the monarchy split into two kingdoms, the northern kingdom of Israel and the southern kingdom of Judah. In the eighth century BCE, the Assyrians destroyed Judah and sent the population to Assyria. In the sixth century BCE, the Babylonians captured Judah and destroyed the Jerusalem temple and sent the Israelites into exile in Babylon. Here, the Israelites learned about Zoroastrianism, which influenced their development of monotheism in service of Yahweh.

When Cyrus conquered Babylon in the sixth century BCE, the Israelites were allowed to return to Canaan. After the name "Judah," the Israelites came to be called Jews.

Jewish religion taught that the Israelites' misfortunes were punishments for breaking their covenant with Yahweh. The development of monotheism was gradual, and the Bible is replete with stories of punishment for worship of deities other than Yahweh. The Jewish people developed an elaborate system of law governing sacred ritual and ethical behavior.
