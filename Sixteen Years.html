Index

The day I shot Steve was the day I handed my life over. I was sure I was done and the shot was merely an admission and a reconciliation to the truth of that situation. But Steve didn’t die so I didn’t get life. I bear the reconciliation to that fact, that I will see the outside again and, even now, am living.

Wednesdays I cook with the other inmates on the tier. We all put in some commissary and come up with something we can share. When I first arrived the creativity impressed me. Life goes on here. Something stirred, the first I’d felt in a long time. One early Wednesday stands out in particular. John is doing life, but his family has stood by him all the same.

“Ruth’s graduating high school next month. She’s going to send the pictures,” John says.

“You should be proud,” Martin offers.

“I am, wish I could be there more than anything.”

I’d only been here for a few weeks at this point, and John and the other guys were just starting to open up around me. John’s reserved delivery was something I felt and noticed and filed away.

John’s bed is decorated with letters and drawings from his children, nieces, nephews. At night he sits and writes long letters to his wife and kids. During the day he works laundry with me. We get paid, though the amount is meagre. We pass the time, sometimes in relative silence, sometimes with idle chatter about his family, the news, inmate drama. In this I started to feel the texture of the place, bleak and lonely as I’d ever been. But I felt something else, too, the small, pitiable warmth that gave the coldness its sting.

On Fridays we have group therapy for our tier. The new guys are always cautious, standoffish. A lot of them just show up for the time reduction or because they’ve been mandated to attend by the courts. The guys who are in here with serious time, who have been coming for a while, use it as a refuge. We usually go over a CBT worksheet that’s on rotation; I’ve done some of them too many times to count, now. One Friday John showed up in a real bad way. We started out going over the sheet but he couldn’t focus. Back on the tier I asked him what was up.

“I’m missing my kids’ lives. Ruth is graduating high school and I won’t be there. I miss my wife. That part never gets easier.”

I didn’t really know what to say so I just listened. He didn’t say anything else really. After a while I went back to my bunk. I saw him looking at photos and letters like he always does.

I laid in my bunk that night staring at the ceiling. I had sixteen years left and then what. I wake up tomorrow and then what. John will never see his family on the outside but he will see the light of day tomorrow. Ruth still loves him and his wife picks up every time. They send him commissary money. He goes to work, to group therapy. Even John looks to his future.

I pulled out the book I’ve been reading from beneath the mattress and try to anchor my attention on the page with little success. The memory of my sentencing churns in my mind. I play the images back. The look on Steve’s mother’s face. The calm of my public defender and the judge enacting the procedure. I feel the same simultaneous dullness and anxiety, spaced out as I was, every bit as resigned as the day I pulled the trigger. I jump ahead, sixteen years ahead, this stretch of time contiguous with the present moment, the anticipatory dread of this moment stretching itself out to cover an amount of time that is hard to even grasp. Functionally, it is an eternity. I roll over and bury my face in the mattress and pull the blanket over my head.

The next morning I rouse from the same troubled sleep as the last day, and the day before, and… half-present, I make myself a cup of instant coffee. The first thought to visit is, again, the sentencing. It’s a memory I can’t resolve, lurking, waiting to command my attention when I’m unguarded. I carry it to my job that day. A quiet day for John, too. He’s got his own festering sores to pick at. That day, out on the yard, I walk in circles. The laps give me something to attach my mind to. The image of the judge’s demeanor coming back over and over has somewhere to land here. The motion feels true to me and it’s here that I feel most at ease. Sometimes I hang back and watch the other inmates shoot hoops, lift weights. This is where they seem most alive, too. I remember one summer looking up at the sun beating down, hot on the asphalt, bringing shadows to life under the trees. I thought of the time on the yard the day before, identical to this one.

That night, John approached my bunk. I think his thoughts were getting to him.

“I got a letter from Ruth. She’s thinking of studying psychology when she goes to college next year.”

“Psychology?”

“She says she’s interested in the way people’s minds work. She’s always been good with people. She said she wants to do something in law enforcement, like being a parole officer or something.”

“That’s interesting.”

John looks at the TV for a moment, then back to the letter he was holding in his hand.

“I’m thinking of trying to get into that welding program or trying to get my GED. Gotta do something with all this time here.”

I nod.

I remember the next night too. The usual procession of local news and then a block of Family Feud reruns. Steve’s mother’s face had left me, the loop finally exhausted itself. A fleeting moment of peace. I laid in my bunk and flipped through a magazine I had read too many times. Boredom drove me to it as a matter of habit but the repetition was draining and irritating; I came to expect some relief through association, but the habit had worn itself out, run its course. I got up and took a shower. After that I had some ramen and resigned my attention to Steve Harvey. This had some effect.

I looked over at John’s bunk and I saw him filling out a request form for information on programs. My attention shifts to the top of his bunk. I think of Ruth sending him letters about her graduation. I think about his wife who calls him on the phone every night. Sixteen years is a long time. A lot can happen to a person in sixteen years. The spell broke, even if only for a moment.
