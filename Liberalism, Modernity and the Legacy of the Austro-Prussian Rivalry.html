Index

The Holy Alliance, formed after the congress of Vienna, was an attempt to return to the old order following the Napoleonic revoltion (Kissinger). The French Revolution, following on the heels of the Enlightenment, was an overt political expression of a nominalist-bourgeois logic. Replacing hierarchical order with direct popular sovereignty, the French revolution was an existential threat to the ''ancien regime'', which Metternich and his contemporaries were at pains to preserve. The old order came under threat from geopolitical developments and their attendant modern ideas. Echoing Schmitt, the struggle for dominance among the German states can be understood not only from a materialist standpoint, but as expressing the development of modern ideas, seen as secularized versions of what were originally theological concepts (Schmitt 36).

The emergence of modern, nominalist ideas has been traced by Gillespie, Milbank and others from William of Ockham through Martin Luther. Ockham’s nominalism, opposing the dominant scholasticism of the time, rejected a realist conception of universals, instead arguing that universals were mere signs. This led to a radical conception of the individual person (Gillespie 19-40). In Luther, this becomes the rejection of the mediation of the church in favor of direct legitimation through God, a form the nation-state inherited (Gillespie 101-128; Schmitt 36). In ''Critique and Crisis'', Koselleck locates the emergence of the Absolutist state in the wars of religion. In particular, he focuses on the philosophy of Thomas Hobbes as a principal articulation of this process. For Hobbes, the equality of individual consciences, conflicting with those of others, causes war. This view of the equality of consciousnesses produces a homogenized individual subject in opposition to an external order. The Absolute sovereign is a moral necessity to impose order on men in the form of law. This divides the private conscience from public adherence to the sovereign. This leads to a private space of morality that develops independently from external state forms. This resulting bourgeois consciousness is the basis of Enlightment morality, once turned outward, that makes its claims in the name of reason. The atomistic, radical individual posited by this line of thought is the basis for the nation-state idea: an association of atomized, federated individuals opposes the feudal conception of legitimacy as being granted through hierarchical participation in a universal (Koselleck 23-40). In Herder, the universal subject of the Enlightenment is particularized as the subject of the Volk; likewise, in Fichte, the subject becomes the Urvolk, the self-legislating nation (Berlin 168-242; Fichte 49-63, 91-108). This tension between liberal Enlightenment morality and the older participatory order can be seen in both the Austrian and Prussian states.

The question of German dualism during the struggle for unification reveals conflicting ideas of political legitimation enacted and engendered by historical struggle: the struggle between the powers is not only reducible to purely geopolitical or economic considerations, but needs to be understood on the level of a conflict of ideas. Two solutions to German unification presented themselves: the Grossdeutsch, or Greater German, solution, proposed the inclusion of Austria and appealed to an older, supranational and dynastic tradition continuous with the Holy Roman Empire. It implied an older, hierarchical and participatory social form. The Grossdeutsch solution emphasized a federalist, dynastic-universal, and Catholic vision of hierarchical participation through sharing in the universal authority: a centralizing power with particular states whose legitimacy was inherited through hierarchical participation in this universal. The Kleindeutsch, or Lesser German, idea, excluded Austria and was more closely aligned with national-statist and Protestant interests. It carried implications of a modernizing, structurally Protestant logic that underpinned the idea of the national state, more closely associated with Prussia. This challenged the unification along ethnic nationalist and, implictly Protestant-nominalist, lines that the Kleindeutsch solution favored: pan-Germanism under the Prussian ideal starts from the particular, national state arranged in horizontal relation to other states that are legitimized directly, through their own assertion, rather than inheriting legitimacy through mediation as in the Austrian model. However, this typical construction was never absolute, and the confluence of older developments and newer ones existed to varying degrees within both states. Though earlier, Austria pursued its own modernizing policies under Joseph II. There were Catholic supporters of the Lesser German solution and Prussian anti-nationalists. Bismarck’s Prussia rhymed with the hierarchical logic of the Austrian state: in the place of previous Habsburg dominance, Bismarck maintained monarchic, aristocratic rule in the outward form or ‘shell’ of a nominalist ethnic nationalism. This is seen concretely in the institutional structure of the Second Reich. Prussia's three-class voting system kept the Prussian Landtag under Junker control, and because Prussia was constitutionally dominant within the imperial federation, aristocratic control of the largest constituent state translated into effective veto power over the Empire despite the Reichstag's universal male suffrage (Berghahn 1-21, 178-184). The strategic implementation of modern forms while maintaining elite power was modeled on the government of Napoleon III, a development that Wehler situates as ‘Bonapartism,’ a feature in the broader ‘revolution from above (Wehler 55-62).’ The appearance was of a parliamentary democracy, but effectively, the old aristocratic class maintained power. For Bismarck, nationalism had instrumental value, as it was useful to consolidate an aristocratic order. This attitude is clearly seen in his strategic manipulation of national tensions in France, Austria and Denmark in the wars of unification.

The Kulturkampf illustrates the challenge Catholicism posed to the Enlightenment underpinnings of liberalism and, by extension, the Protestant values of the Kleindeutsch tendency. The Vatican I declaration of papal infallibility occurred during the same year as Germany’s unification, a rival claim to sovereignty (Ross 1-30). Catholics were perceived as not only harboring loyalty to a foreign power, but as a revolutionary threat to the political order in Prussia. Catholics were seen as disloyal or regressive: fantasies of Jesuit conspiracy and disdain towards perceived Catholic superstition exemplifies the perception of Catholicism as opposed to progress and reason (Gross). The Kulturkampf was not a simple failure of liberal tolerance: the structure of ecclesial authority was fundamentally opposed to the legitimating structure of the nation-state.

An inversion of the Prussian situation, Austria saw rationalizing reforms earlier under Joseph II while maintaining the outer appearance of its older dynastic form. Joseph II sought to subordinate the church to the state and institute a centralized modern bureaucracy with German as a common language (Judson 32-49). While Germany was united along ethnic nationalist lines under monarchic rule, the older Austrian centralization was undermined by ethnic conflict, encouraged by the Josephine reforms (Kann 332-388). In Prussia, centralization under a nationalist form strengthened the aristocracy considerably, whereas in Austria the modernizing efforts undermined the dynastic system. Josephine enforcement of German as the common tongue produced backlash among ethnic minorities within the Austrian state. This came to a head in the 1848 revolutions, which were suppressed. The tension was deferred to the compromise of 1867, the formation of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, but not resolved. This was an unsuccessful compromise that attempted to preserve both the dynastic nature of the Austrian state and appease nationalist sentiment (Kann 332-388). The opposition between nationalism and the older dynastic situation is inverted in the Federal State of Austria, which can be seen as trying to resolve the universalist and nominalist problem in reverse: opposed to the nationalism of Germany, it shared the nationalist ground from the Austrian side. But in affirming Austrian nationalism, it valorizes the dynastic past that is unique to Austria. Specifically, The Federal State of Austria drew on the ‘universalist’ and corporatist ideas of Othmar Spann and other Romantics, as well the Catholic encyclicals ''Rerum Novarum ''and ''Quadragesimo Anno ''(Spann; Leo XIII; Pius XI)''. ''Under Dollfuss, an Austrian nationalism opposed to pan-Germanism developed, while simultaneously reorganizing the state internally along the lines of corporatist ideas and Catholic social teaching (Diamant 131-140, 256-279). In this way, the Austrofascist state embodied an external nominalism and an anti-nominalist core. Nationalism and dynastic existence are fused in a modern corporatist system. This conflict comes to its completion in Hitler’s Anschluss, an inverted universalism where the particular, national category of the Volk reinscribes the earlier hierarchical order in a racialized form: a development Voegelin analyzed as a secularized reduction of Christianity in the form of a political religion (Voegelin 19-73). The Volk concept is universalized and made immanent, with a stratified hierarchy of races replacing the great chain of being (Lovejoy) of the earlier dynastic idea while preserving the structure. The gradated system of participation in which a hierarchical order definitional of an individuals remains, but the estate system is swapped for a racial hierarchy defining differential participation based on blood quantum; the concrete institutions of the racial state-- the Party, the SS, the Hitler Youth-- replace the monarch; and the Nazi state, through efforts such as the Nuremberg rallies, created a transcendent, metaphysical order that served the purpose of the earlier Catholicism (Mosse 73-127).
