It's a cold Illinois winter and I'm coming down with a cold. In order to avoid staying at the shelter all day, I've been working out at a restaurant in town. I stopped at a new spot today because I like novelty, and going to the same places every day gets old pretty fast. It's a Sunday and the last bus leaves from the closest stop at 7:05 tonight.

I've had a very stressful day and I'm struggling mentally and emotionally. After praying some of the imprecatory Psalms, I decide to read Luke, finishing the first four chapters. I take pause at the story of Zachariah: God silences him for his lack of faith after promising to grant Elizabeth the birth of John. This, of course, foreshadows a contrast with Mary, "blessed is she who believes." The birth of John foreshadows the yet greater miracle of Jesus' birth. I finish on the three temptations of Jesus in the desert. Meditating on this, I reflect on my own struggles with trust and faith.

I go out ten minutes before the bus arrives. Waiting in the cold always raises my stress level; I start praying Hail Marys to center myself. I am always nervous that the last bus won't show, which has happened often enough here to give basis to my worst-case scenario thinking. In addition, I don't usually take this route home, and while I know where to get off, it's dark and I'm worrying I'm going to miss the stop. The bus here usually does not announce its stops, leaving me to rely on the landscape to know if I've arrived. These piling anxieties lay atop my illness and exchaustion, flooding my nervous system with adrenaline and cortisol.

A row of lights approaches that signal either an early arrival or a truck: I try my best to discern which, in fervent anticipation. I think it's a bus, but it turns out to be a truck, but the foreshadowing of Jesus by John is on my mind and, sure enough, the bus arrives soon after. Now I am focusing on the anxiety of missing the stop; finishing my Hail Marys, I had been meditating on Jesus' three temptations in the desert. The route on the bus signals a route that stops closer by the shelter than the other bus. Google Maps did not list this as a stop for that particular route, and I feel relieved before realizing there was probably a route change and the sign had not been switched over. I've passed my first trial; shockingly, and I want to convey that this is, in fact, highly unusual for the buses in this area generally, the bus is announcing stops over the speaker. Another wave of relief, and another trial: I am not certain I remember the designated label for the stop. I try to check Google Maps, but I am having difficulty getting it to cooperate. Instead of persisting, I fall back to trusting that my memory will not fail me and watch the stops out the window. When the stop is announced and I, in fact, recgonize it, another wave of doubt hits and I end up at the end of the line. But God did not leave me stranded; the final buses for the night in the opposite direction are leaving in 15 minutes. One bus also stops right by the shelter.

It's funny how God communicates with us in ways that we find interesting and particularly tailored to our understanding. Reading the Scriptures analogically and, too, the experiential aspects of my own life, can lead to some astounding experiences.
