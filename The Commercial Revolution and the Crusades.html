The Commercial Revolution and the Crusades

The rise of a money economy profoundly changed European society and provoked widely different reactions among people and in the culture. The new cash economy drove changes in the church, the development of new monastic orders and the crusades.

The Commercial Revolution

Increase in population size, cities, trade networks, local markets and novel businesses all led to the development of an economy centered around profit. Innovations in agriculture created a food surplus that was able to support an urban population. The commercial revolution helped create new kinds of institutions that are still in existence, in modern form, today. These include use of money, corporations and accounting systems.

The revolution in commerce was seen in fairs and markets; people would travel regularly to sell their wares in these venues. Landlords profited greatly from these institutions, and as the fairs came under royal control, so did kings. Cities and towns formed around castles and monastaries, becoming permanent fixtures, leading to profit for estate owners. In the tenth century, lords reorganized their lands and collected payments in cash from peasants, fostering the development of markets and encouraging traders to settle in cities and towns.

Many long-distance traders were Jewish and Italian, providing luxury goods to lords and ladies. Italians were located in a fortuitous location, and Jews had been involved in commerce since the time of the Roman Empire. While Jewish trade was initially centered in the Mediterranean, changing little for Jews in the area, northern expansion of trade changed the lives of Jews living in Europe. Driven off their land in the tenth century, they found refuge in towns and took up professional occupations. By the eleventh century, most Jews lived in cities, despite lacking citizenship; generally speaking, they were serfs or under the protection of bishops. Jews were both protected and exploited by these relationships. Jews and Christians lived side-by-side and engaged in the same world.

Almost all cities included a marketplace, a castle, and multiple churches. Residents lived in crowded conditions. With the development of roads, merchandise could be moved in carts rather than by boats, nonetheless, the fastest-expanding urban centers emerged in areas with access to waterways.

Most goods were produced by hand or with simple machines and tools. However, most crafts were organized in a guild system. Guilds were religious and charitiable groups of people working in the same industry. Guilds charged dues and set industry standards. Finished products often required the cooperation of different guilds to produce. Each guild was organized hierarchically, with apprentices at the bottom and masters at the top. It was difficult to achieve the rank of master, requiring many years of work in the trade.

New types of business arrangments, including partnerships, contracts and large-scale enterprises, were developed in the emerging capitalist system. All of these organizations brought laborers together to work towards a common initiative. The form these arrangements took varied across Europe. Sharing resources required the availability of money. Coinage was important, but credit was also necessary. Entrepreneurs needed to take out loans to fund their projects. The church's ban on usury inspired creative ways to get around the prohibition, such as contracts specifying a penalty for a late payment, rather than directly charging interest. Contracts made the development of light industry possible, and these new industrial technologies changed the production of cloth and led to the invention of the water mill. Machines were developed to make more efficient use of raw materials in the form of mining technology, and use of iron became widespread, further increasing productivity.

Communes

The social organization of the townspeople did not reflect the earlier division among those who prayed, those who fought, and those who worked, creating a new kind of solidarity. Townspeople petitioned for representation in the form of officials and law courts, wanting self-governance. This led to the development of the commune, a legal corporate body representing the interests of the townspeople.

In the countryside, trade had no less impact on social organization. Rural life, too, was centered around the marketplace. Lords hired hands to manage the estates, calculate profits and help with marketing decisions. Many nobles went into debt trying to stay ahead of increasingly wealthy townsmen. The lord's need of cash helped integrate the peasants into the emerging economy. Population increases required more land, and villages were founded on newly cleared land with peasants sharing in work responsibilities.

Population growth strained the older organization of the manses, leading to many families living in close quarters. Peasant services and dues were converted into money rents. Peasants gained from price increases, making fixed rent more manageable. Access to markets allowed them to sell surplus and attain needed items. Increases in iron assisted productivity. Peasants gained greater freedom from their lords.

Church Reform

The church, too, was profoundly affected by the commercial revolution. Kings typically appointed bishops in exchange for gifts. In the tenth century, people began to condemn these transactions, reformers demanding separation from secular authority. People increasingly took issue with secular authority having a role in the church; this came to a head in the Investiture Controversy, a conflict between Pope Gregory VII and Emperor Henry IV. The ensuing civil war in Germany reorganized the distribution of power in Europe. By the early 1100s, the church had a staggering and unprecedented amount of authority in everyday life.

Church reform began in the tenth century. The monastic community of Cluny was foundeed in 910, endowed with property by the duke and duchess of Aquitane. Rather than maintaining control over the monastery, it was given into the hands of Peter and Paul, designating the pope as its earthly protector. Cluny was a very prestigious monastery that received many land donations. Many other monasteries reformed on the lines of Cluny. The Cluniacs advocated clerical celibacy and reform amongst the laity and, in particular, took an interest in the poor. A small group of clerics and monks took up the cause of the Cluniacs, focusing on two particular practices: simony and lay investiture, the buying and selling of church offices and the appointment of clerics by lay rulers, respectively.

Emperor Henry III was very committed to the reform ideas of the Cluniacs; he refused to accept gifts when appointing bishops, denouncing simony. In 1046 three different claims to the papacy were made and Henry traveled to Italy to resolve the issue. All three popes were deposed and a new pope elected, appointing the bishop Leo IX. Leo set out his own program of church reform, not following Henry. The power of the pope expanded and Leo comdemned bishops guulty of simony. During Leo's last year serving as pope, he sent the reformer Humbert to argue against the patriarch of Constantinople on his behalf. The patriarch reacted contemptuously, leading Humbert to excommunicate him. In retaliation, the patriarch excommunicated Humbert. Following this incident, the Orthodox and Catholic churches were split.

To the south, Leo faced another threat to his power; Count Roger I of the Normans had created a county that would come to span from Capua to Sicily. Leo attempted to curtail its power by sending an army to Apulia, but he was defeated. Leo's successors had to modify their policies: they gave, despite not having the power to do so, Apulia, Calabria and Sicily to Roger's brother. The pope hoped to bring the area under Catholic control, resulting in the agreement of 1058 that promised the conversion to Catholicism. When the Investiture Controversy broke out, the Normans would fight on behalf of the pope.

The papacy was becoming increasingly involved in military affairs, participating in the wars of expansion of Spain. Fragmentation led to wars of expansion and, eventually, the reconquista, an attempt to win Spain back to Christianity from Islamic rule.

Gregorian Reform and the Investiture Controversy

The papal reform movement is most associated with pope Gregory VII. Gregory came to loggerheads with emperor Henry IV, ruler of Germany and much of Italy. Gregory eventually reached the conclusion that the king was just another layman who had no place in deciding the affairs of the church. For the time, this position was novel, to say the least. Gregory remains controversial to this day: he thought himself acting as St. Peter's earthly representative, freeing the church from the world. Henry IV saw Gregory as a ruthless, ambitious and unholy man. Henry did not accept papal primacy, seeing bishops as the rightful leaders of the church.

The Investiture Conflict began in 1075 over the appointment of an archbishop at Milan. Henry insisted on appointing the bishops, an action Gregory contested. Henry told Gregory to step down as pope. Gregory, in response, called a synod to excommunicate Henry. Gregory attempted to spark a revolt by insisting that no one serve him as king; Henry's enemies, having the legitimation of the pope, threatened to elect a new king. Some bishops backed the pope, a blow to Henry. Defeated, Henry traveled to meet Gregory; they met in a castle, where Henry stood outside in the snow begging forgiveness. Gregory was bound to lift the excommunication. Henry's opponents elected a rival king, and Henry elected a rival pope. A long war between the crown and papal power ensued.

The Investiture Controversy was resolved in 1122 with the Concordat of Worms, a compromise. Henry V gave up the right to confer spiritual power, but retained the right to be present for the election of bishops, ensuring continued influence over the vote. While largely symbolic, the roles of kingship and priesthood were cleaved. More importantly, the controversy had changed the political landscape in Germany and Italy. In Germany, princes, who had opposed Henry IV, now held more power than the king; in Italy, the road was paved for the development of self-managing communes.

The Effects of Reform Among the Laity

The perception of church officials following the Investiture Controversy had changed. The role of the papacy changed, church law had been systematized anew, inspired novel monastic orders dedicated to poverty and initiated the crusades. The sacraments were the means by which grace was infused into the natural world, and they began to take on a new importance. After the Gregorian reform, marriage became a sacrament, whereas previously the church had little role in this arrangement. The sacrament of the Eucharist also began to take on a central role in the Catholic faith. The emphasis on the sacraments and the desire to enact clear distinctions between the priesthood and the laity led to vigorous enforcement of priestly celibacy. This exacerbated the schism between the Eastern and Western churches further, as the church in the East did not practice priesthood celibacy.

Bishops further distinguished themselves from the laity through the adoption of an elaborate manner of dress meant to underscore their holiness. Consolidation of church power was enabled by imposition of canon law. The church, possessing vast swaths of land, had made the pope into a monarch. Around 1140, a teacher of canon law by the name of Gratian wrote a synthesis called the Decretium. He sought to analyze the words of the popes and church fathers and show their underlying agreement.

New Monastic Orders

The Cluniacs and other Benedictine orders were reformers. The so-called "black monks" inhabited monastaries structured by elaborate daily ritual in equally elaborate churches. These monks came under fire by reformers who emphasized the importance of poverty. The Carthusian order was an example of this new sort of monasticism. Another group were the Cistercians, led by St. Bernard. St. Bernard became abbot of Clairvaux, a Cistercian monastary in Burgundy. These monastaries spread rapidly throughout Europe. While following the rule of St. Benedict, the Cistercians came to their own distinct style of monastic living. Their churches featured understated architecture, a reflection of their commitment to poverty. Cistercians spent time involved in monastic administration in addition to private prayer; each house had its own large farms, and monks and nuns would spend large amounts of time tending to livestock and their estates. Despite their commitment to poverty, the Cistercians were not spared participation in the life of the commercial revolution. The Cistercians placed a large emphasis on emotion in their spirituality, centered on an intimate relationship with Jesus and Mary.

The Crusades

Pilgrimages and holy wars resulted in the crusades. The crusades followed in a long tradition of sanctioned holy wars. The crusaders established Christian states in the middle east that lasted for two centuries.

The arrival of the Seljuk Turks in Asia Minor kicked off the first crusade. The Islamic world was fragmented at this period of time; weak, these Islamic states were easy targets for the Seljuks, who adhered to Sunni Islam. The Seljuks captured Baghdad and displaced the Abbasids, become a serious threat to Byzantium. Romanus IV, the Byzantine emperor, struggled to come up with the manpower to oppose the Seljuk Turks, demonstrating how weak Byzantium had become. Romanus relied on a mercenary force comprised of Normans, Franks, Slavs and Turks. This force met the Seljuks at the Battle of Manzikert in eastern Anatolia. The Seljuks routed the army and captured Romanus, ending the domination of the Byzantine Empire in the region.

Settling in Asia Minor, the Turks expanded across the region, spanning all the way to Jerusalem. In 1095, the Byzantine emperor Alexius I begged Pope Urban II for assistance. Urban offered an indulgence, or the forgiveness of sins, for anyone who would make the journey. Urban wanted Christian control of Jerusalem. Eager to curb violence, he hoped to turn errant knights into valuable soldiers for Christendom. Urban's initiative offered a new leadership role for the church.

The First Crusade

People of all walks of life heeded Urban's call, setting off on the First Crusade. 60,000 to 100,000 people left their homes to travel to Jersualem. Fighting for God, the acquisition of land and plunder, or to follow a lord, many made the excruciating trip.

The armies were not a united military force, but rather comprised of several militias, each commanded by a different apointee. The forces also included irregular armies. Failing to heed the words of the pope, some of these armies departed early. These irregular forces traveled through the Rhineland, eager to kill Jews, who did not accept the divinity of Jesus. The crusaders let loose on the Jewish people of the Rhineland, many dropping out after this exertion of energy. The rest continued through Hungary to Constantinople, where they were shipped off to Anatolia. Most of the arrivals died, and the main crusader armies did not arrive until later.

The Turks spared the crusaders, considering their weakness. The crusaders managed to organize themselves and defeat a Turkish army, then surrounded Nicea and took the city. The crusaders continued on to Antioch, and one army took the city of Edessa. The main crusaders then took Antioch. They followed this by marching to Jerusalem. Muslim infighting made their task easier, and the crusaders took Jerusalem.

Crusader States

By 1109, the crusaders had created several small states in the Holy Land. The crusader states functioned as lordships: rulers gave fiefs to vassals. The crusader states gave birth to a new kind of monasticism: that of the Knights Templar, who vowed themselves to poverty and chastity; unlike other monks, the primary concern of the Knights Templar was military warfare.

The Second Crusade

The Seljuks took Edessa in 1144, the first blow that led to the decline of the crusader states. This event led to the second crusade. The second crusade was poorly planned and organized, quickly suffering blows delivered by the Turks. Near the end of the crusade, the leaders agreed to storm Damascus. Meeting outside the city, the crusaders quickly abandoned the attack. The crusade led Louis VII, a supporter of the endeavor, to divorce his wife, the heiress of Aquitane. She quicly married Henry, who would become king.

Impact of the Crusades

Eight major crusades occurred before the end of the thirteenth century. The crusades suffered from European unwillingness to commit to provding the vast amount of resources that the crusader states needed to survive. The crusades had a minor role in stimulating trade and may have prompted the development of the modern system of taxation. The crusades worsened Islamic fragmentation.

Return of Monarchies

Money and the commericial economy enabled greater effectiveness and higher revenues for kings, who would develop new ideologies to legitimize their rule.

Ten years after the Battle of Manzikert, Alexius Comnenus became Byzantine emperor. From a family of dynatoi, his rule saw overwhelming Turkish and Norman aggression. In addition, the people of Constantinople were struggling with high taxes and cost of living. Alexius built an army of mercenaries, dynatoi and knights paid with promises of land and granted the urban elite new offices.

As a result of the Investiture Controversy, Henry IV had much less power over the church and Italy than his father did. German princes rebelled against his rule and cities in Italy declared independence. After the death of Henry V, the role of the emperor was uncertain. Lothar III was chosen as the next monarch, but died before he could consolidate control. He was succeeded by Conrad III, whose nephew Frederick Barbarossa would find new sources of imperial power.

Norman Rule in England

In the twelfth century, English monarchs were the most powerful in Europe, ruling by right of conquest.
