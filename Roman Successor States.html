Roman Successor States

Islam

Islam emerged in Arabia among sedentary people who inhabited oases in the desert and later spread among the nomadic Bedouins. Islam led to the unity of faith and governance by religious and political leaders known as caliphs.

Born to a merchant family, the prophet Muhammad retreated to a cave where the angel Gabriel revealed the Quran, establishing Islam, which means submission to God. Muhammad's faith was rejected by the tribemen at his home in Mecca, leading him on the hijra, where he established a religious community in the settlement of Medina. In part, Muhammad was drawn to Medina due to the large Jewish population, in which he expected to find eager converts. However, on this point he was disappointed. During this time, additional religious obligations, including the zakat, or taxes on possessions for alms, fasting during the Ramadan season, the requirement of hajj or pilgrimage to Mecca, and salat, worship three times daily, were instituted. During the Battle of Badr, Muhammad established rule at Mecca, defeating the opposing tribesmen. Now turning against those who refused to convert, Muhammad expelled two Jewish tribes, and changed the direction of prayer away from Jerusalem, towards Mecca.

Muhammad was succeeded by a series of caliphs. The first two caliphs proceeded without much controversy, but by the time of the third caliph, contestations arose over the rightful heir to Islam. Some Muslims were unhappy with the distribution of offices and suspected favoritism. A rival, Ali, the husband of Muhammad's daughter Fatimah, challenged the caliph; Muslims following Ali became known as Shi'ites. Ali lost the struggle for the caliphate, ushering the Umayyad dynasty.

Islamic states, while culturally and religiously diverse, maintained unity through, espeically, the common language of Arabic. Islamic society featured open borders and relative tolerance of non-Muslims. For example, the Tustari brothers, Jewish merchants from the south of Iran, established business in Egypt. They occupied the highest rank in Jewish society, and maintained trade relations with the Muslims.

Byzantium

In the seventh and eighth centuries, Byzantium was facing significant external threats from the Persians and Germanic and Muslim groups. Busy with the Persians, peoples including the Lombards and Bulgars captured territory and forced the Byzantines to recognize their newly-formed independent kingdoms. Byzantium shrunk in size and saw the decline of urban life, undergoing a process of ruralization. Society was reorganized into military districts called themes. With the decline of urban life, education moved away from a pagan, classical focus to being more religious in nature.

In the eighth century, Byzantium was torn apart by the iconoclast controversy. Images had gained a particular prominence in worship, which the emperor felt as a threat to his power: images functioned as an intermediary between God and worshipper, undercutting the emperor's authority. Seeking to weaken the power of the monasteries, Byzantine emperors ordered the destruction of icons. In addition, the influence of Islam suggested to the army that Byzantine failures were attributable to God's wrath over their idolatry.

Western Europe

Western Europe during the Merovingian period was more fragmented than Byzantium, which still operated under the rule of an emperor. Power in Merovingian Europe was distributed among various kings. The elite tended to live in rural areas, cities being a shell of their former Roman glory. Peasants and aristocrats tended to live together in villages. Economic activity was mostly centered around a gift economy, with the wealthy amassing goods in exchange for tribute, then donating to religious institutions, who would distribute the donations. Trade was, however, carried out in a limited form with Byzantium in particular. The climate of Western Europe, combined with inadequate agricultural technologies, made it difficult for the land to support the size of the population.

Merovingian elite culture reflected warrior values, and the most powerful groups of people included monks and bishops. Marriage was an important institution and required a great dowry being gifted to the bride. Nonetheless, women found ways to exercise power, often behind the scenes. The Merovingian kings sat at the top of the aristocracy, and were successful in maintaing their power due to their alliance with the lay aristocracy and church authorities. They fused Roman culture with culture of the conquered peoples.

The city of Tours and, in particular, St. Martin, was important to the Merovingians; the shrine of St. Matrin was used as a symbol legitimating their rule. In addition, Tours was in a strategic location, sitting between northern and southern territories. It thus served as a center for trade.

Christianity in the British Isles

The Irish were early converts to Christianity, bringing their religion to Scotland. English Christianity came from two directions: the Irish in the North and from missionaries south of England. Pope Gregory the Great sent missionaries that divided England into administrative units along the lines of the Roman church. This form of Christianity could be at odds with the Irish variety. Irish and British Christianity provided the seed for a flourishing intellectual and literary culture, as reading became important in order to practice the liturgy and read the writings of the church fathers. Anglo-Saxon Christianity adapted to Anglo-Saxon pagan traditions, including providing liturgy and writings in the common vernacular. The decision of the English church to follow Roman tradition over Irish tradition occurred in the Synod of Whitby, prompted by a debate over a difference in the dating of Easter.

Spain and Italy

When the Merovingian king Clovis defeated the Visigoths, their kingdom was dismembered; by the middle of the sixth century, the Franks ruled over most of their territory in Gaul. The Visigoths in Spain, being Arian Christians, could not secure the loyalty of the Hispanic peoples, who followed Roman Christianity. The Visigothic rulers ended up converting to Roman Christianity, and the Arian bishops followed. This brought unity to Spain. This centralization made it easy for arriving Muslims to conquer the kingdom, as the only necessity was to defeat the king.

The Lombards in Italy, on the other hand, struggled with disunity, and the official religion of the state depended on who was in power at that particular time. Conversion to Catholicism was gradual, and the Italian kings never fully won the support of the church due to this fact.

The Pope

Around the year 600, the pope's position was ambiguous: he both had spiritual authority but was nonetheless subject to the Byzantine emperor. Pope Gregory the Great laid the foundation for papal power. Under Gregory, the church came to possess vast amounts of territory in Italy. He used his wealth to fund Rome's army. In addition, he provided charity, heard legal cases and made treaties. Gregory was responsible for sending missionaries to England, converting them to Roman Catholicism. Eventually distance from Rome and possession of large amounts of land gave the church de facto power in Italy beginning in the seventh century. In the eighth century, under Emperor Leo III, this gap between Byzantium and Rome grew. Emperor Leo imposed taxes on church property in order to fund a war against the Muslims, resulting in papal revolt. In addition, the iconoclasm of the emperor clashed with the pope's approval of icons.

Centralization and Fragmentation

In the mid-ninth century, conflict emerged over competing loyalties to emperors, kings and caliphs versus loyalty to regional rulers and family. Issues of internal diversity arose. Starting off with a period of increasing centralization and stability of rule, the end of this period would see a lapse back to fragmentation and decentralization. During this period, Byzantium saw a cultural renaissance in Constantinople, concurrent with the emergence of a new elite in the countryside. Islam would see the replacement of the Umayyad dynasty with the Abbasids, and the capital would move from Damascus to Baghdad. This move symbolized the break with the Umayyads and the inclusion of Persian Muslims. In addition, Baghdad lay in an advantageous location from a trading perspective. The Islamic world, too, underwent a cultural renaissance. In Western Europe, Charlemagne would unite the disparate kingdoms and usher in the rule of the Carolingian dynasty; Western Europe, too, would see a cultural renaissance. However, Charlemagne's kingdom would come to a demise shortly after his death. New states on the border of the former Roman empire, such as the Rus, would emerge.

Byzantium

Staving off Islamic invasion, Byzantium expanded from 750 to 850. While a cultural revival was flourishing, power would shift to emerging regional leaders in the countryside, undermining the power of the emperor. The themes remained, but Byzanotum also saw the formation of new armies called tagmata. These armies would push outward, conquering territory for the Byzantines. By 1025, the empire spanned from the Danube to the Euphrates. Soldiers gained prestige and the empire gained revenue from building estates on conquered territory. A lavish court culture emerged. An agricultural economy oriented to trade made some emperors very wealthy; Byzantine economics was a careful balance of state-managed guilds and private enterprise. Byzantine trade flourished in the Middle East and Western Europe, as well as in the Rus.

The Macedonian Renaissance

Under Basil I, a revival of intellectual and artistic culture flourished in Byzantium. Basil's dynasty drew on families who had persisted in studying the classics, and this elite once again would see power. The patronage of the emperor subsidized commentaries on classical literature and encyclopedias. One of the most memorable innovations of this time, demonstrating the merging of Christian and classical culture, emerged in the illuminated manuscript.

The Dynatoi

Powerful military families emerged outside of the Byzantine capital, forming a new hereditary elite called the dynatoi. Rich from plunder and wealth gained from landowning, they exercised significant local power. This led to a distribution of power resembling fragmented Western Europe, where land was worked by peasants subordinate to a lord.

Eastern Europe and the Kievan Rus

From 850 to 950, the contours of what would become modern Eastern Europe emerged. Slavic settlements populated the areas from the Danube to Greece and from the Black Sea to Croatia. The Bulgarians controlled the largest swath of territory. The Byzantines began to conquer Slavic territories during this time. The Byzantines conquered the area and began the conversion of the Slavs from Paganism to Christianity. By 900 the Byzantines had conquered all of Greece. Under Nicephorus I, the Byzantine Empire launched an offensive against the Bulgarians. Reaching a truce until the time of Basil II, the Byzantines then began another campaign of conquest, forcing the conversion of the Bulgarians. Opposing the Bulgarians, the Serbs formed what would become Serbia. The mass conversion of the Slavs to Christianity led to the development of Cyrillic, an alphabet for writing the Slavic languages based on Greek forms, enabling the Slavic peoples to read the Bible.

In the ninth century, the Vikins would conquer the area beneath the Gulf of Finland, imposing their rule over the Slavic peoples. Rus and Byzantium began a relationship centered around trade, leading to war and conquest, and the eventual conversion of the Slavic peoples. This was part of a broader pattern of Christianization in Europe. Byzantine Christianity dominated the east, and Roman Catholicism the west.

The Islamic World and the Fall of the Abbasids

The Abbasids first brought centralization to the Islamic world and then its descent into fragmentation.

In 750 a civil war broke out, bringing the Abbasids to succeed the Umayyad dynasty. The Abbasids were composed of an alliance between Shi'a muslims and non-Muslim peoples. The Abbasids moved the capital from Damanscus to Baghdad. The Abbasids ruled firmly, bringing centralization and stability to the caliphate. After the death of the caliph Harun, the Abbasid dynasty began to decline, and the Islamic world lost control over much of its territory, including Syria and Egypt. In response, the caliphate sought support from the outside groups, notably, the Turks. These converts were called Mamluks, and were initially brought in as slaves, but once captured were released and paid a salary. The Abbasids did not have the tax base to pay the Mamluks, and the Islamic world fell into fragmentation, much like in Byzantium.

The Islamic world was fraught with internal contradictions based on religious and ethnic divisions, leading to a decentralized form of administration under regional rulers. For example, the Shi'ite group called the Fatamids established rule in Tunisia and Egypt. This period of rule lasted around 200 years. While Egypt was Shi'ite, the Sunnis dominated al-Andalus, or Muslim Spain. In al-Andalus, Christians were allowed to practice their religion so long as they paid a tax levied on non-Muslims. The dynasty ruling over al-Andalus was called the emirate of Cordoba, and its fragmentation led to the formation of taifas, small, independent regions of rule.

The Islamic Renaissance

The fragmentation of the Islamic world meant that the Islamic cultural renaissance was located in multiple centers, including Cordoba. The focus of scholarship was diverse; some focused on ancient philsophy, others on astronomy and mathematics. The Islamic world had centers of higher learning before Europe.

Western Europe Under Carolingian Rule

Like the Islamic and Byzantine worlds, Western Europe saw centralization under a leader followed by fragmentation and decentralization of power. Charlemagne was the most powerful Carolingian ruler. Under Charlemagne, Western Europe saw a renaissance of its own.

During the Merovingian period, the Carolingian family came to monopolize the position of palace mayor. Charles Martel gave the dynasty its name, proving his might battling against the Muslims of al-Andalus. Martel and his family successfully weaponized aristocratic conflict, rewarded their supporters and crushed their enemies. Martel took control of entire regions by supporting monasteries that served as centers of religious piety and land donations. The Carolingians allied with the papacy, earning the support of Boniface, who appointed bishops loyal to the Carolingians. Martel's son sealed Carolingian power officially by turning to Pope Zachary to legitimizde his kingship. Later, when the pope required assistance against the Lombards, he turned to the Carolingians for help; this marked the turning point from the papacy's orientation towards Byzantium to the West. The Lombards formed a peace treaty with the pope after their defeat by the Carolingians, sealing the autonomous region of papal power that had been effectively established long before. The Carolingians' good relations with the papacy gave their reign an aura of religious legitimacy.

Charlemagne was the most powerful Carolingian king. He dreamed of unifying Western Europe in a way that honored both its Roman and Germanic heritage, as well as Christianity. During the early years of his rule, Charlemagne conquered territories in every direction, invading Italy and securing the crown from the Lombards, then moving north against the Saxons, followed by a campaign against the Avars and the Muslims of al-Andalus. Naar 800, Charlemange's empire stretched from modern-day Germany to Austria, through Spain and Italy. He sponsored building programs and intellectual and artistic projects. In order to prevent corruption, Charlemagne appointed officials known as missi dominici to oversee regional governers.

In the meantime, the papacy was exploring its own imperial ambitions. A forgery known as the Donation of Constantine was produced, declaring that the pope was the rightful inheritor of "all provinces, palaces and districts of the city of Rome and Italy and the regions of the West." This led to tension with the Carolingians and Byzantines. Pope Leo III, accused of perjury and adultery, nearly escaped being blinded and having his tongue removed. Rescued by Charlemagne, Leo crowned him, hoping to demonstrate the right of the papacy to appoint imperial power.

The Carolingian Renaissance

Like the Islamic and Macedonian renaissance, the Carolingian renaissance saw a revival of classical learning and art. Carolingian scholars and artists drew on Anglo-Saxon scholarship and illustrated Bibles, in addition to laying the foundation for later monastic schools.

Successors of Charlemagne

Louis the Pious imposed strict Benedictine rule on the monastaries of the Western empire. Hoping to ensure the unity of the empire, he made his son Lothar co-ruler and his other sons sub-kings; should he die, only Lothar would inherit the crown, preventing fragmentation. When Louis' wife died, he remarried and had another son by his second wife, Judith. With the birth of a second heir, his other sons rebelled and infighting ensued. When Louis died, the Treaty of Verdun split his empire in three ways. Charles the Bald inherited the Western half, to become France; the eastern part went to Louis the German, to become Germany; and the middle part to Lothar, which was to become the Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, Switzerland and Italy. Thus, Charlemagne's empire had fragmented. The loyalty of the aristocracy had held the empire together, united under Charlemagne's Christian ideal. Once the conquests ended, the aristocrats could not hope for further material gain and amassed their own followings, undermining the unity of the empire.

The Carolingian economy was based on war profits, trade and agriculture. In the north, the economy overlapped with that of the Abbasids. Silver likely entered the Islamic world from the Volga to the Baltic Sea. Coins were melted down and traded for Carolingian goods. The Carolingians used this silver to make their own coins. The fall of the Abbasids disrupted the Carolingian economy through the collapse of trade. Land, being the most important source of Carolingian wealth, held vast estates called manors. Peasants lived on these estates and worked the land for wealthy aristocrats. Peasant family organization prefigured the nuclear family. The most progressive form of plowing, called the three-field system, involved farming two-thirds of the land in fall with wheat gathered during the winter, one-third in the spring with the summer harvest, and leaving one-third fallow. The crops were rotated such that land use only repeated every three years. This system produced larger crop yields than the older two-field system.

Invasions

Around the time of Charlemagne's crowning through the tenth century, Vikings, Muslims and Magyars conducted frequent invasions of Western Europe. The Vikings held occupations as merchants, sailors and pirates, some crossing the Atlantic in longships to settle in Iceland and Greenland. A pagan people, Christian institutions were not sacred and were seen only as a valuable target to plunder. Britain was hit particularly hard, and some Vikings settled in the area. In Weesex, King Alfred the Great appeased the Vikings by offering hostages and tribute, which would form the basis for a later system of taxation. Alfred eventually led an army against the Vikings and invasions subsided.

The Vikings set up trading posts in Europe and settled. One Viking settlement in France became Normandy. The leader, Rollo, eventually converted to Christianity, making Normandy a Christian polity. Scandinavia became the Christian kingdom of Denmark under the influence of missionaries sent by Charlemagne, and Denmark set up powerful monarchies that came to control parts of Sweden, Norway and even England.

Southern Europe, while safe from Vikings, was subject to Muslim threats. Meanwhile, the Magyars, a tribe from Central Asia, settled in Central Europe. The Magyars conducted extensive raids to the West, threatening Germany, Italy and southern Gaul. In 955, the German king Otto I took on a band of Magyars and won. He subsequently reorganized the army and ended Magyar invasions. The Magyars eventually converted to Roman Christianity.

Post-Carolingian Western Europe

With the decline of royal power, welathy aristocrats looked elsewhere to sustain and increase their wealth. They began to command allegiance from vassals and establish castles, controlling the peasantry, establishing markets and enforcing peace, becoming regional rulers. Powerful men commanded their own private armies to ensure loyalty, granting them fiefs in a relationship of vassalage in return for military service. These positions tended to be hereditary. This system was called feudalism.

Society in the middle ages was divided into three groups: those who prayed, those who fought, and those who worked. Monks and knights enjoyed freedom, despite being embedded in a network of dependencies, as peasants were. Vassals frequently had their own vassals. Vassalage held society together in the absence of centralized, public power. Vassalage bonded lord and liege together in a relationship of mutual dependency. Peasants increasingly lost their status as free, becoming bound to lords as serfs. Most people in the middle ages were peasants.

Improvements in agricultural technology and warming climate helped the population of Western Europe to expand. The expansion of the population, in turn, led to further improvements in agricultural technology and a food surplus, leading to improved living conditions. Some lords instituted fixed money payments, lightening the burden on the peasants and enabling the lords to open up new lands. By the tenth century, many peasants began living in villages, fostering a sense of community. The church was often the focal point of village life. Communal life among the peasantry fostered a common sense of purpose and identity. Obligations varied among peasants between different regions of Europe. In France, power became so fragmented that every castle owner, called a castellan, was effectively a ruler. The development of independent political units controlled by a castle hosting a military elite completely changed the European societal landscape.

Warfare in Western Europe

At the top of the warrior hierarchy were kings, counts and dukes; below them, castellans; and beneath these, ordinary knights. Knights and their lords engaged in combat on horseback with lances, a revolution in military technology. Lords and vassals often shared a residence. They ate meals together in the halls, shared in entertainment, and slept in the same place. They hunted together. Powerful vassals rarely saw their lords, being masters of their own fiefs. Unmarried knights were a new phenomenon in Europe, ushering in changes in how inheritance was handled: primogeniture, or the eldest son being named heir to property, was a new development. Heritage was traced through the male line. Younger sons were left to live in courts or enter religious life.

Italy, England and France

Political systems varied across polities in Europe. In Italy, the city was still the dominant form of political organization, as it was in Rome. Italian lords built castles inside cities, controlling the people in the surrounding countryside. Italian cities facilitated commerce, where peasants and merchants could sell their wares. Peasants eventually became renters, helping meet the lords' need for cash payments. Italian families were different from the patrilineal families of France; male members formed a sort of corporation, sharing the family's inheritance. Italian businesses would inherit this model of organization in the future.

Most of England was rural; Alfred the Great of Wessex further developed monarchic systems of government and instituted a navy. Alfred also sought to strengthen religious unity, subsidizing scholars to live in his court and translate the works of Church fathers. He had several works translated into vernacular English. Alfred's reforms strengthened royal power in addition to defense, education and religion. He curtailed the power of the Danes and consolidated control over Wessex. He issued a common law code for all of England.

France, being much larger than England, struggled to control the invasions, not having time to slowly build their defenses. During the tenth century, Carolingian rule alternated with Capetian rule. As Carolingian decline continued, Hugh Capet was elected king. In the eleventh century, lords controlling their various territories limited the power of the Capetian dynasty. The king's estates and castles were centered in the northern area of France, excercising relatively limited power, yet enjoying great prestige.

Central and Eastern Europe

Late in the Carolingian period, five duchies were formed in Germany. One of the dukes elected themselves king after the last Carolingian king died. As Magyar invasions increased, the kingship was given to the Saxon Henry I, who reorganized his army and established fortifications. Henry I's son, Otto I, defeated the Magyars at Lechfeld, winning him great prestige. Staving off Slavic invasions, the pope crowned him emperor in 962 and claimed the middle kingdom set out in the Treaty of Verdun. He called his new polity the Holy Roman Empire, hoping to continue the legacy of Rome.

The Ottonian kings-- Otto and his successors-- were not always able to live up to expectations, despite great success. The Ottonians instituted a patrilineal succession system as seen in France, leading to resentment among younger sons, who led a revolt. Otto I had good relations with the church and bishops, granting bishops the right to collect revenue and raise armies, leading to bishops becoming royal officials.

The Ottonians oversaw a cultural and intellectual renaissance.

Kings of Germany, despite their power, saw resistance from dukes and other princes. The Salians, following the Ottonians, designated certain specific men to serfdom, called ministerials. These men collected taxes, kept the peace and fought on horseback. The popes and German kings created new Catholic states in the east, convering the Czechs, Poles and Magyars.
