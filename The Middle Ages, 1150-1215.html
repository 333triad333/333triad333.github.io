The Middle Ages, 1150-1215

These years saw the development of the Gothic cathedral and the birth of the university.

Schools and Churches

The development of the Gothic style and a renewed emphasis on learning paved the way for later developments in the middle ages.

Since the time of the Carolingian dynasty, schools had been connected to the church. Students flocked to cities to learn liberal arts. The liberal arts curriculum was subdivided into three courses: the trivium, consisting of grammar, rhetoric and logic; the quadrivium, consisting of arithmetic, geometry, music and astronomy; finally, students could continue on to study medicine, theology, or law.

Around the year 1200, the pope referred to masters of theology, church law and liberal arts as a universitas, Latin for guild or corporation.

Gothic Architecture

Gothic architecture succeeded the previous Romanesque style. Romanesque architecture was oriented towards prayer, which frequently took the form of communal chant without instrumentation. Gothic architecture, by contrast, reflected the mentality of the cities and merchants. Gothic churches were the focal points of cities. Characterized by pointed arches, vaults and stained-glass windows, the Gothic style spread from churches to other art forms. Gothic architecture was designed to appeal to the senses, leading them to the transcendent.

The Gothic style began with the project of Abbot Suger, who was tasked with remodeling portions of the church of Saint-Denis. Decorating the entrance of the church with figures of Biblical kings, queens and patriarchs, Suger emphasized the connection of the king to his glorious past. In the eastern wing of the church, Suger added pointed arches and stained glass, allowing worshippers to access the light of heaven.

Government

During this time, government was become institutionalized; kings were not just rulers of people, but of territories. Interest in Roman law served centralized rule. Money allowed kings to hire government officials. This process occurred first in England.

English Common Law

Officials in England handled governance even in the king's absence. The king's frequent visits of other areas in Europe necessitated an administration. Henry II was the monarch behind this development of English bureaucracy. Henry II took power in the midst of a brutal civil war between two royals, a dispute that empowered barons and high churchmen with new powers and privileges. Henry would reverse this. Henry had a tremendous amount of power; his marriage to the duchess of Aquitane gave her inheritance to the English crown. In addition, she provided him with heirs. Previously married to Louis VII, she divorced him, but lost much of her power in the marriage to Henry, who dominated her. When Henry II became king, he destroyed and confiscated the new castles the barons had acquired in the civil war. He then extended royal power through judicial reform. Under English law, Anglo-Saxon kings had their own district courts. Henry built upon this scaffolding with the introduction of eyres, a system in which royal officials would travel across England to judge accused criminals. These officials summoned knights to inform upon criminals violating the king's peace. The eyres also heard civil cases, including interpersonal disputes and matters concerning inheritance and property claims. This formed the basis of English common law, or law that applied to all of England.

Hnery's greatest opposition came from the church, who had their own judicial process. Reluctant to submit to Henry's courts, leading to a conflict between Henry and archbishop Thomas Becket, the greatest dispute between church and state of the twelfth century. Becket would not allow accused clergy to appear in Henry's court, leading Henry to murder Becket. This had the unintended consequence of Becket becoming a martyr.

Henry's frequent travels made his presence felt everywhere in England. He used war and negotiation to maintain his power. The omnipresent court system's wealth came from court fees, feudal dues and income from a vast collection of estates. Henry's successor, Richard I, embarked on the third crusade the year he attained kingship. He was captured and died shortly after being released from captivity. Richard's son John saw the diminishment of the English empire, with France confiscating their northern territories. He demanded exorbitant dues from his military and feudal dues, which he used to fund the army. Despite this, he was defeated at the battle of Bouvines. In 1215, John was forced to agree to the Magna Carta, a document defending the rights of the nobility, outlining clear obligations and rights. The king was forbidden from violating the precepts of the Magna Carta, making the king subject to law. The Magna Carta would come to be seen as guaranteeing the rights of ordinary English people.

France

Philip II benefited from the territorial confiscation of John's lands. Henry II and the counts of Flanders and Champagne attempted to control Philip, who played the rulers against each other, taking Flanders, Normandy, Anjou, Maine, Touraine and Poitou from John. This acquisition made Philip the wealthiest and most powerful ruler in France. Philip wrote down his decrees, a novel practice in French governance at the time.

Germany and Frederick Barbarossa

The Investiture Controversy had weakened the power of kings to the benefit of German princes, who enjoyed near-independence. The princes ensured that any king they elected would further add to their territories and powers. Conflict of interest between royal offices, families and the princes, as well as the empowered papacy, limited the ability of kings to consolidate power. Germany was decentralized and favored the interest of the princes.

The Investiture Controversy was dominated by two noble families representing opposing sides in the conflict. On the imperial side was the Staufer clan, the papal side was the Welfs. The rivalry between these two families raged even after the Concordat of Worms. Eventually wanting peace, both families agreed to elect Frederick I (Barbarossa), whose mother was a Welf and father a Staufer. Frederick subordinated the princes, turning them into his vassals, despite granting them near-royal power. When he arrived in Rome to be crowned emperor in 1155, the commune protested. Frederick insisted that he had authority by right of conquest, giving him imperial right over the city. Frederick was influenced by Roman law and smilutaneously convinced of the sacred nature of the kingship. He married Beatrice of Burgundy, which gave him significant power in Swabia.

Frederick's ambition caused problems in Italy: the communes opposed him and the pope considered Italy to be under his jurisdiction. Eventually, though, Frederick managed to conquer northern Italy by 1158. He decided to appoint outsiders as magistrates, a characteristically Italian solution; however, he appointed German officials unfamiliar with Italian political traditions. These officials managed to create great resentment. Northern Italy joined with the pope to create the Lombard League against Frederick. Defeated, Frederick withdrew from Italy. This battle marked the victory of the Italian commune, and Italy would not have centralized rule until the nineteenth century.

Frederick made decisions based on an outmoded social order: he tried to bound German princes as vassals at the moment that the commercial revolution was underminding feudal organization. He tried to rule foreign territories according to German traditions and failed. He also struggled in Germany, where princes effectively acted as local rulers. The growing power of his vassal Henry the Lion led Frederick to drive him out Germany, which lost him the needed support of other nobles, forcing him to divide his territories amongst them.

Fragmentation in the Rus and Byzantium

In Eastern Europe, the pattern of rule followed leadership under one king, whose authority dissolved under a successor. For example, in Hungary, King Bela III built up the trappings of a European state, even marrying a French princess. However, he did not manage to set up the sort of instutions needed to perpetuate his system following his death, and his kingdom dissolved. Experiences in the Rus were similar.

The Byzantine Empire was also displaying signs of weakness. Western traders dominated the economy, and the emperors disempowered civil servants, elevated imperials to higher offices and showed favoritism towards the military elites. Byzantium came to increasingly resemble Western Europe. During the fourth crusade, Constantinople was captured by a crusader and named emperor. Though the crown was won back, the Byzantine Empire would never recover its old strength.

Vernacular High Culture

Poems and songs began to be written in vernacular for the first time in Europe. Troubadour poetry was one such expression of vernacular high culture. Courtly love, or love for an unattainable, higher status woman, was a recurring feature of troubadour poetry. Another genre written in vernacular was the epic poem; the value of chivalry was seen in the figure of the gentle knight, influenced both by the epic and romance genres.

The vernacular culture reflected the growing wealth and confidence of the 12th century in addition to the religious embrace of urban populations. Spirituality was intimate and emotional, as seen in the primacy of the sacrament of the Eucharist.

Urban Religious Orders

One of the most successful new urban religious orders was that of the Franciscans. Francis of Assisi began to experience emotional and spiritual trouble that spurred him to become a monk. Francis placed a high value on poverty and preached outside of monastic settings. Francis, according to his biographers, received the gift of stigmata as a grace. Francis was a powerful speaker and attracted many followers, called mendicants. The pope recognized the mendicants as a religious order, committed to labor, preaching and caring for the sick. Eventually the Franciscans would disperse and set up fraternal organizations around Europe.

One of Francis' followers, Clare, formed a women's order called the Order of the Sisters of St. Francis. The church came to disapprove of their activities, and the sisters were confined to Benedictine cloisters instead. Laywomen who lived religious lives in community were called Beguines. Beguines chose celibacy and lived ecstatic emotional and spiritual lives. At first, the Beguines were tolerated by the church, unlike other minority religious ideas branded as heresies. The most prominent heretics were dualists, including the French Albigensians, or Cathars.
