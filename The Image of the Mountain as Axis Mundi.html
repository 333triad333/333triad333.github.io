The Image of the Mountain as Axis Mundi

The symbol or image of the mountain in Abrahamic faiths is a complex, composite idea and motif that accrued multifaceted meanings over time through historical inheritance. This image first appeared, in the Western context, in ancient Near Eastern traditions. The mountain simultaneously serves as axis mundi, a symbol of ascent, and as a representation of God. In this image, process and telos collapse into one symbol. This “fusion” or, more correctly, lack of alienation of the component ideas, can be seen in early Near Eastern writing, and becomes more fully elaborated in Neoplatonic interpretation. In this paper I will attempt a genealogical and symbolic-metaphysical analysis of this image.

According to Mircea Eliade, sky worship is a prominent, cross-cultural feature of many early religions. The sky serves as the limit of the mundane world and is aspirational to something greater or transcendent. The meeting of the mundane and spiritual worlds in a cosmic center, or axis mundi, is another common feature. The axis mundi is frequently symbolized as a mountain (Eliade 99). The cosmic mountain appears in early Near Eastern texts, including the Ugaritic Baal Cycle and Enuma Elish. In the Abrahamic faiths, the role of the mountain-image inherited certain functions from early Near Eastern religion, giving it a complex and multifaceted function in Abrahamic spirituality. It is simultaneously the axis mundi, or the center of the world and meeting-place of the mundane and spiritual worlds, an image of God himself, and the completion of the teleological, ‘backwards’ ascent to God, or the culmination, process and goal of spiritual ascent. Mount Sinai and, later, Zion, function simultaneously as the goal of spiritual ascent and its completion in the Jewish and Christian mystical tradition. The mountain is God’s home, his temple, and the consummation of return, acquired upon completion of ascent, which collapses, again, into unity: in the collapse of process and goal, the mountain is united as God and ascent to God. Motion and difference are illusory, or hierarchically derived from the Neoplatonic One. This same duality can be seen from another angle, as the collapse of the visible, ‘empirical’ world into a hidden, ‘spiritual’ one that is experienced as being ‘full of meaning.’ This subjective experience of theopany can be seen as a collapse of the multiple into One. This idea is, again, most associated with a Neoplatonic frame of reference, but as I will attempt to demonstrate, has precedent in earlier Near Eastern traditions, which later deepened through Neoplatonic interpretation.

The palace of Baal in the Ugaritic Baal Cycle presents a fused image of Baal with the temple, located on Mount Zaphon, and with Mount Zaphon itself. The identification of Baal with the temple occurs imagistically in the following passage from an Ugaritic fragment:

"Baal sits (enthroned) like the sitting of a mountain,

Haddu . . . like the (cosmic) ocean,

In the midst of his mountain, divine Sapan,

In [the midst of?] the mount of victory,

(With) seven lightning-flashes,

Eight store-houses of thunder (?).

A tree-bolt of lightning.[..]

His head is adorned (?),

With dew between his eyes

. . . at his base,

. . . the horn[s] . . . on him (?),

His head with a downpour from the heavens

. . . is watering,

His mouth like two clouds (?). . .

Like wine is the love of his heart. . ."

In the above fragment, Baal/the temple acquires visual features of Mount Zaphon, displaying its ‘composite’ nature (Smith 65; CAT 1.101). Mount Zaphon is at once temple, Baal, heaven and axis mundi. Likewise, in the Babylonian epic Enuma Elish, Marduk’s dwelling shows these features of the undifferentiated concept. Succeeding the defeat of Tiamat, which engenders the separation between heaven and earth, Marduk’s dwelling is referred to by the Akkadian word hursanu, which has been interpreted to mean “mountain,” “the place of the river ordeal,” or “the ordeal (itself)” (Enuma Elish IV.93-146; Clifford 24). In this way, Marduk’s dwelling in the ziggurat becomes identical with the ziggurat itself, Marduk inhabiting the process of the ordeal and the ordeal itself. This is fused with the image of the mountain as axis mundi: a complex, composite image already emerging that is drawn out by later Neoplatonic layering. The process of ascent is collapsed into dwelling in the mountain-temple, revealing time, movement and struggle as illusory, or subordinating becoming in time to eternity. Baal is displayed through the temple, through the mountain: one ‘sees’ sacred reality in and through the formal expression.

The covenant at Sinai in Exodus 19, too, establishes Sinai as axis mundi:

"16 On the morning of the third day there were peals of thunder and lightning, and a heavy cloud over the mountain, and a very loud blast of the shofar, so that all the people in the camp trembled.

17 But Moses led the people out of the camp to meet God, and they stationed themselves at the foot of the mountain.

18 Now Mount Sinai was completely enveloped in smoke, because the LORD had come down upon it in fire. The smoke rose from it as though from a kiln, and the whole mountain trembled violently.

19 The blast of the shofar grew louder and louder, while Moses was speaking and God was answering him with thunder.

20 When the LORD came down upon Mount Sinai, to the top of the mountain, the LORD summoned Moses to the top of the mountain, and Moses went up.

21 Then the LORD told Moses: Go down and warn the people not to break through to the LORD in order to see him; otherwise many of them will be struck down.

22 For their part, the priests, who approach the LORD must sanctify themselves; else the LORD will break out in anger against them.

23 But Moses said to the LORD, The people cannot go up to Mount Sinai, for you yourself warned us, saying: Set limits around the mountain to make it sacred.

24 So the LORD said to him: Go down and come up along with Aaron. But do not let the priests and the people break through to come up to the LORD; else he will break out against them.

25 So Moses went down to the people and spoke to them."

In Ezekiel 28, we can see the fusion of the mountain, temple and Eden:

11 The word of the LORD came to me:

12 Son of man, raise a lament over the king of Tyre, and say to him: Thus says the LORD God: You were a seal of perfection, full of wisdom, perfect in beauty.

13 In Eden, the garden of God, you lived; precious stones of every kind were your covering: Carnelian, topaz, and beryl, chrysolite, onyx, and jasper, sapphire, garnet, and emerald. Their mounts and settings were wrought in gold, fashioned for you the day you were created.

14 With a cherub I placed you; I put you on the holy mountain of God, where you walked among fiery stones.

15 Blameless were you in your ways from the day you were created, Until evil was found in you.

16 Your commerce was full of lawlessness, and you sinned. Therefore I banished you from the mountain of God; the cherub drove you out from among the fiery stones.

17 Your heart had grown haughty because of your beauty; You corrupted your wisdom because of your splendor. I cast you to the ground, I made you a spectacle in the sight of kings.

18 Because of the enormity of your guilt, and the perversity of your trade,you defiled your sanctuary. I brought fire out of you; it devoured you; I made you ashes on the ground in the eyes of all who see you.

19 All the nations who knew you are appalled on account of you; You have become a horror, never to be again."

Israel is cast from the mountain and temple, recapitulating their explusion from Eden and, later, exile (Levenson 110). The analogical correspondence with Eden underscores Sinai-Zion as depicting a state of unity with God.

Plotinus conceived the practice of purification as a movement, inward and backwards, to the One (Plotinus I.6.8-9). Reality emanates in degrees, outward, that can be visually conceptualized as distance from its source (Plotinus V.1.6-7). The goal of purification is to return to the center, for outer forms to collapse and return to One (Plotinus VI.9.7-9). One can see the mountain as axis mundi in this image: Earth, or the realm of empirical, separated existence, is derived hypostatically from the One, and the end of purification is to re-unite in the center, or Heaven (Plotinus V.2.1-2). The meeting of heaven and earth seen in the axis mundi is the goal and only ‘truly’ existing thing, the One and its emanations, which are inseparable in actuality.

Gregory of Nyssa conceives God as essentially incomprehensible, following his predecessor Philo. The negative theology of Philo and, especially, Gregory, holds that God is unknowable in essence, an epistemological orientation that shapes the phenomenology of ascent. For Gregory, the ascent of the mountain leads into an ever-deepening darkness, as what is comprehensible to the senses and intellect is progressively cut off as one nears closer to God. Spiritual ascent occurs in three stages, and is split between an active moment and contemplative moment (Louth 81). For Origen, the soul is brought to increasing perfection in its increasing sight; for Gregory, the moment of illumination occurs first, in order to draw one into increasing unknowing (Louth 79; Gregory of Nyssa II.162-4). Gregory of Nyssa presents a process of unending, forever unfinished becoming: because God is infinite, we can forever ascend, growing further and further into unknowing darkness (Louth 93; Gregory of Nyssa II.231-3, 238-9). Here, too, process is identified with its end: we can ascend indefinitely because God himself is endless. In pursuit, we are participating in the ascent to God. But this becoming is both yearning and satisfaction in the eternal consummation of that yearning, in the process of growing closer to God.

Dionysius the Areopagite formalized Proclean-Plotinean triads and the chain of ascent into a formal, hierarchical structure that, when read mystically, provides a ‘grammar’ of spiritual progression. This cosmological gradation is prefigured in Enuma Elish. In Enuma Elish, a chain of deities are created out of the undifferentiated being, Apsu (Lambert, 193-194; Enuma Elish I.1-68). According to Lambert, the Babylonian text KAR 307 describes a division of three heavens and three earths in the Babylonian universe (Lambert 197-198; KAR 307). In Enuma Elish, the universe is divided into a tripartite structure by Marduk (Lambert, 196-197; Enuma Elish IV.93-146), corresponding to locations associated with the earlier divisions of the pantheon. This structure is common to the Biblical creation account found in Genesis, with the days of creation being structurally parallel to the procession of the gods/division of the cosmos in Enuma Elish. In J Fokkelman’s scheme, the six days of creation in Genesis can be broken down into two three-day periods, with the first two days in the first triad comprised of inanimate creation, or creating space to be inhabited; this is mirrored, in a ‘complementary’ and inverted fashion, in the second triad, which has one day dedicated to creation of inanimate creation and two dedicated to creatures. Days 1, 2 and 4 correspond to the heavens, and days 3, 5, 6 to earth. Neoplatonic thinking would later provide an emanationist cosmology, being fused with the graded cosmos found in the Genesis myth and personified by the hierarchy of angels, reflecting the triadic structure of the creation account.

Days 1-3: Formation, Separation Days 4-6: Filling
Day 1: Light separated from darkness Day 4: Sun, moon and stars
Day 2: Waters separated, sky/firmament created Day 5: Birds filling the sky, fish in the seas
Day 3: Waters gathered; dry land, vegetation appears Day 6: Land animals and humans

Ascent in the Plotinean system occurs through reflection on cause:

"Its knowing is not by search but by possession, its blessedness inherent, not acquired; for all belongs to it eternally and it holds the authentic Eternity imitated by Time which, circling round the Soul, makes towards the new thing and passes by the old. Soul deals with thing after thing — now Socrates; now a horse: always someone entity from among beings — but the Intellectual-Principle is all and therefore its entire content is simultaneously present in that identity: this is pure being in eternal actuality; nowhere is there any future, for every then is a now; nor is there any past, for nothing there has ever ceased to be; everything has taken its stand for ever, an identity well pleased, we might say, to be as it is; and everything, in that entire content, is Intellectual-Principle and Authentic Existence; and the total of all is Intellectual-Principle entire and Being entire. Intellectual-Principle by its intellective act establishes Being, which in turn, as the object of intellection, becomes the cause of intellection and of existence to the Intellectual-Principle — though, of course, there is another cause of intellection which is also a cause to Being, both rising in a source distinct from either."

At the moment that Being is discerned by the Intellectual-Principle, the cause of the operation and, thus, existence of the Intellectual-Principle becomes Being, as the Intellectual-Principle exists, teleologically, for the sake of Being. Thus there is a reflection or inversion of causality that meets in the hypostatic act, an inward movement towards the One, collapsing an ‘interior’ and ‘exterior’ view. The ‘complementarity’ or hypostases of the Genesis triads are reflected in Dionysius’ angelic hierarchy. The elaboration of chain of ascent via Proclean triads gives, again, grammatical expression to spiritual progress. Again, human ascent of the mountain coincides with God’s descent, the two directions meeting in the center where all differentiation again collapses into unity. Appearance fully expresses reality: at this moment, one can see the divine fused, fully present in the world of the senses.

In the Christian tradition, the Sinai-Zion symbolism came to be seen as culminating in the symbol of the crucifixion. Sinai, the site of the covenant with Moses and, later, Zion, converges typologically with the image of the Temple and the garden of Eden, as seen in Ezekiel 28; all serve as a type of Christ. As Origen argues, the cyclical rise and fall of Israel over the centuries served as progressive revelation that was only understood as such after the crucifixion (Origen IV.1.6-7). Christ united with his cross, dying and at once being reuinted with the Father, reconciles the division between heaven and earth, making redemption eternal and omnipresent (Origen II.6.3-4). Through death, one achieves eternal life: the ascent is identified with the consummation, and external forms communicate the higher, invisible one ‘inside’ (Origen II.3.2-3). The Son reveals the Father, which is being eternally born as the Son at every moment through the procession of the Spirit (Origen I.2.4-8). The Eucharistic miracle makes real all that can be seen, said or experienced; it is complete, God’s final and only word.
