Squat and trapezoidal, the building's architecture appeared, to him, to resemble a ziggurat. Here, the silt accumulates in layers, becoming heaven and earth. Here they come together, where he lives, writing his research on Assyriology, steady.

Inside. Phone rings: it’s Will’s mother. Will, she says. Have you gotten out lately, I think it would be good for you to get out. Can you help me. I have an errand, she says. This intrusion is unwelcome, makes him uneasy. Already, attention scattered, not looking good for him. He says, knowing his mother’s predilection to, in an uncanny way, suspect when he’s on the verge of it, and make the call, that he’s quite busy. But, she protests. I can’t get out in this weather, I’m not young like I used to be so I really can’t do it myself. Besides, she says, you shouldn’t be inside so much, I’m worried about you. Sighs. Ok, what is the errand? Can you pick up some milk from the store, it is so cold outside, bring it over please? He agrees, unfortunately, knowing what’s next.

So he descends the steps. Outside it’s a mess, eroding winter in its fullest expression. Every icy breeze an assault, especially the slow ones, the slow, deep, rage-inducing wearing-downs, chipping at his resolve. The recognition is immediate, he remembers yesterday and the day before, anticipating struggle and quickening its coming. The recognition makes itself actual. Molasses-like trudging to the bus, like a dream walking struggling through the water, tiny impingements by atoms, he pictures, tiny tiny sharp cold, dry atoms. The terrible, barbed particles tear away smally at his senses, an affront to morale. Going to get milk, trained myself for this, he braces for thousands of small impacts just skating beneath the surface of acknowledgement, barely seen, fast-moving, sensations intended to undermine. Unfocused, he goes on, what else, anyways.

Cars swiftly passing straight through, and you can’t see one at a time, you can’t fix it in place, hold it, examine it. Together a raging torrent, blaring loud, wheels spinning: ever-closing approximations to something past already, trying to hold or put together the pieces, dissolute and the dissolution extended, scattered around and torn to shreds. Enlil asserts his terrifying might, Will’s eyes wide, barren of Anu’s ordering temperance. The river floods, the Elamites assault consciousness and coherence, everything in ruins and wondering why, despairing, and how much longer. Roaring bubbling forth through the intersection, hard to make sense of it all.

Lucky: it comes together. The regular traffic patterns, the stabilizing effect of stoplights and crosswalks. The cars, yet no less terrifying in their power, impress him with a kind of awe when the perception coheres, chaos permeated with structure, inseparable organization from its material. A feeling as if pondering the immensity of the sky, destructive, incomprehensible mass set to order. He thinks of his work, already homesick.

As one might predict, the bus arrives, rudely. He clambers on, taking a seat in the back by a window. It’s moderately crowded onboard and he watches the people today. Busy with their phones, scrolling, texting, crushing the barley with the pestle. The phone scroll has a kind of rhythm. Routine, repetitive and familiar, controlled and controlling. He looks over out the window, taking in the passing scene, white falling in zigzags. It’s easy to feel the soft, slow falling. This, too, feels even, ordered. The length to the store is one continuous attempt to apprehend the view, outside the window and the people, too, what would give that feeling of realizing it. He strains for the thought that elevates the scene to comprehension and totality, wanting to see, again. It’s a waiting game. You do some thinking, let it go, relax, then wait for the unconscious to come back with something. Sometimes he feels like the soil is too full of salt, barren; reworking the soil again and again offers diminishing returns. He’s not convinced of his mastery of crop rotation.

Frustrated, arriving at the store and anger rising. It’s busy, crammed, lights blaring, offensive in the utmost; long long lines trailing from the register. He starts walking towards the rear of the store. Five steps in, a person is blocking the aisle have to go around, weaving in and out bubbling, frothing and overflowing, he maneuvers deftly, swiftly, through the crowd. His phone rings and it’s his mother. Will, she says, did you make it to the store. Half there half not, and tells his mother that he is near done. Good, she says. The call concludes but its impression lingers. The impression pressed itself on the situation. He is living attendant to anticipation of the next moment and every experienced felt second is stretching towards the end, the only thing he can see is the whole thing, the whole thing so far in the distance making each second a strain, an exertion, the pressure of the moment oriented towards the end and passing imperceptibly slowly. Let’s get this over with. He speaks the word to himself. The phone call is over but he feels the lingering effects ever-present, it’s impressed itself on the present. All of this circling while he walks to the back. Many more people to dodge, dodge that thought, stop. He tells himself to stop. As he quickly tries to purchase and exit.

At the back of the store someone’s in front of the milk freezer. He eyes them. Waits. He wants to cast his giant net, to shoot the arrow straight into the heart of Tiamat, to separate the heavens and the earth establish his temple, the final victory, the leaving, walking out, his mind is drawing images of walking out the front door and then backwards, fantasizing about the register, all while he waits behind this woman at the milk freezer as she takes her time, time that seems to stretch out and drag to eternity, in pain. Eventually and finally, she closes the door and walks away.

Got the milk victorious and relieved. The winding ascent the return victorious. He can set the world in order victorious. Momentous momentarily until the next victorious. Heaven and earth separate, Marduk’s everlasting temple erected. Yet, even now, he still knows that the river is capricious, inscrutable.
