2: What is Tim Crane’s proposed definition of religion, and what do each of its four elements mean? How does his definition relate to two other necessary elements of religion, namely the “religious impulse” and “identification”? Finally, what is the “religious temperament,” and how is it distinct from the “religious impulse”?

Crane’s definition of religion has four components: religion is systematic, it is practical, it aims at finding meaning, and lastly, it seeks the transcendent. The first part, its systematicity, means that there is an organized belief structure that locates the human in relation to the transcendent ideal. It provides practical codes for moral behavior and ways of living that are drawn from its sacred stories, designed to guide one closer to the religion’s goal. This is related to ritual, another practical expression of religious piety. Ritual and moral behavior are a way of bringing theology into everyday life, or making the transcendent present and real. One is aligning one’s life and world with the transcendent order, growing closer to its source. Meaning is found in the everyday practice of religious faith, and again, is found when the transcendent is revealed, connecting one’s actions and experiences to this supernal source, infusing ritual or moral reasoning with the presence of the transcendent.

What Crane calls the “religious impulse” is the tendency to perceive a hidden order behind the ordinary world. He goes on to draw a distinction between the normal and the normative, distinguishing between the scientific search for knowledge and the religious search for meaning, perhaps to preserve rationalism from charges of religiosity. From a historical perspective, however, the two are less cleanly separable; the human impulse to understand the world was, from its inception, inseparable from the desire to answer questions that satisfy the need for meaning and, even more explicity, to draw closer to alignment with the transcendent ideal. Further, secularist metaphysics bear the stamp of their religious heritage, perpetuating theological concepts in a degenerated or disguised form.

Crane uses the term “identification” to describe the sense of belonging to a religion as opposed to simply believing in its precepts, underscoring the communal nature of religious belief. Finally, Nagel’s conception of the “religious temperament” describes a set of psychological traits and predispositions that orient one to an essentially religious view of the world, even if one is not a follower of a specific faith. As Crane notes, there are certain atheists who possess a religious temperament. In contrast, the “religious impulse” describes something closer to a specific, concrete framework or an explicitly stated or adopted system of belief. Crane uses the example of someone who outwardly participates in a certain faith, but lacks any real relationship to the transcendent in order to clarify the distinction between the religious impulse and the religious temperament.

3: Vine Deloria jr. and Melissa Pflug both consider the human being in relation to the nonhuman socio-cosmos. How does each describe the living of a good life according to Indigenous Religions with respect to one’s relationship to the broader nonhuman world?

Indigenous religion is very communally-focused. In some ways, morality is always, ultimately, about the good of the greater religious or human community, but in Indigenous religions, it has a particular impact on their metaphysical suppositions. Concepts such as pimadaziwin, described by Pflug as right relationships between nature, the community and the individual, start from a non-dualistic, non-conceptual cosmological view of the world. The person is embedded in the world and community inseparably and inextricably, and what is good for the individual is good for the outer world, which is animistically inhabited, everywhere and in everything, by the unity of, for example, the Great Spirit. The individual is not separate from the world, a way of existence that is difficult to capture in the language and ways of thinking of the Western metaphysical mode.

Living a good life is, again, a matter of acting in the best interests of nature and the community. Vine Deloria Jr. emphasizes the reciprocal nature of Indigenous cosmology, lacking a hierarchical, dualistic conception of values so natural to Western religions like Christianity and even the preceding paganism of Western peoples. Relationships with other people and the gods are horizontal, while Western dualistic thinking presumes a hierarchy even in its basic division of presence and absence.

4: What is the Axis Age, and what kinds of shifts in Hinduism occurred during this period? How does the Axis Age relate to the history of the Vedas, the caste system, and shifting understandings of the fundamental purpose of the Hindu practitioner?

The Axis Age was a period of widespread social change in India that was reflected in developments in and reactions to the earlier Vedic form of Hinduism. During the Axis Age, a second period of urbanization was occurring in the area around the Ganges Valley, leading to the development of a new middle class called the vaishyas. The rise of this new class in the urban setting led to a certain degree of democratization, and subsequently, questioning the dominance of the brahmins in the traditional caste system. Hinduism came to reflect the change in social organization of the age, in particular with the emergence of the Upanishads and a more inward-looking form of spirituality dissociated from the former, more ritualistic form of practice. In particular, spiritual focus moved away from participating in ritual sacrifice towards the realization of brahman, or the ultimate reality underlying appearances. Concepts like brahman, atman and moksha became central features of Hinduism, accompanying a shift in social values from collective ritual duty to a more individualistic focus on personal morality, along with a morality that emphasized behavior in line with values of compassion and nonviolence.

5: How are the concepts of Atman, Brahman, Karma, Samsara, and Moksha related? How should one live one’s life in order to reach Moksha, and what sort of mindset is necessary to get there? Use resources from Molloy, the Upanishads, and the Bhagavad Gita in your response.

During the Axis Age, widespread questioning of Vedic spirituality led to further development of Hindu metaphysics. The Upanishads emerged during this time and introduced many new concepts into the religion. Brahman refers to the supreme, unified reality “behind” the veil of appearances, or maya. The supreme goal of Hindu spirituality is to reach a state where all the outer, superficial adornments of the person are stripped, leaving only brahman in pure form. This state is called moksha. All humans partake in brahman, and the aspect of brahman inside of the person is called atman. In pursuit of this goal, a person will undergo rebirth repeatedly; this cycle is called samsara, and whether one moves up or down in the next life depends on karma, a law of cause and effect that repays moral behavior with reward and immoral behavior with punishment.

The Bhagavad Gita is a story of a war; in Hinduism, the story of Arjuna allows one to view spiritual progress as being akin to a battle, requiring discipline, strength, intelligence and courage, and unity of wisdom and action. The perspective of achieving moksha as a process of spiritualized warfare is deeply informed by the Indian caste system. Arjuna, a ksatriya, represents action and, guided by Krishna’s wisdom, represents an ideal of unified action and wisdom. Acting morally means to follow one’s dharma, or to borrow a fitting Nietzschean turn of phrase (not originally on the subject of Hinduism, of course), to “become what you are.” Acting rightly means fulfilling one’s proper end, and understanding what this is ties intimately to the caste system. This is achieved through yoga, or practices that bring one closer to moksha.

6: What are some central similarities and differences between Hinduism and Buddhism concerning their cosmology/metaphysics, the ultimate goal of the religion, and the path to achieving that ultimate goal.

Buddhism inherited concepts from the earlier Vedic religion, especially ideas on the soul, karma and nirvana. Samsara, the word for the everyday world of change and suffering, remains the same in Buddhism as it is in Hinduism. The goal of attaining nirvana is very similar to the Hindu goal of moksha, or release from the endless cycle of reincarnation. While Buddhism teaches that there is no eternal, unchanging essence like brahman, a positive assertion in the constance of change still attests to a kind of metaphysical absolute. The attainment of nirvana, or release from samsara, could be seen as analogous to realizing brahman, or attaining moksha. Nirvana, like brahman, is a state beyond any conceptual categorization, attained through a gradual shedding of attachments or adornment of the self. The path to nirvana in Buddhism is attained through the following of the Eightfold Path, whereas in Hinduism the path is through yogic practice.

2: Each religion we have talked about so far has a framework for the self-in-relation to the world, or what it is that humans are and what that means for how we move through the universe. How does each religion (Indigenous religions, Hinduism, Buddhism) conceptualize the self in relation to the whole? What does this self-in-relation imply about how we ought to live our lives, according to each tradition?

Crane’s four-part definition of religion includes a relationship to the transcendent that gives meaning to the world and the human place in it. The conception of the self and the world originate, fundamentally, in the stories told and what they mean. Religious practitioners internalize this organization, these sacred stories giving form and shape to the individual’s internal life, emotionally and intellectually. Metaphysics and cosmology delimit the boundaries of what can be thought, felt, said and done, and the mode of perception allows groups of people to communicate and live with one another through the mediation of shared understanding. This is externalized in ritual and tradition, which further, reciprocally, shape individual internalization and morality.

In Indigenous religions, the self is inseparable from the world, and this lack of clear boundaries is reflected in Indigenous cosmology. The self is interpenetrated by the world and the world by the self: the view of the role of the person is ecological, having a definite place and function within nature, rather than the atomized individual separated from the world of Western philosophies and religions. The concept of pimadaziwin, or right living, communicates that this view of the self and the world lends itself to a prioritization of the good of the whole as the ultimate standard by which to judge the morality of an action. The whole encompasses not just self, but community and nature.

Vine Deloria Jr. emphasizes reciprocity: this ethic is demonstrated by certain self-limiting behavior where one does not take more than what is needed, making sure to leave nature and the community intact enough to continue to provide for itself and the person.

In contrast, Hindu cosmology enshrines a certain dualistic mode, between brahman and atman, or the divine in the world and the divine in the self. The goal of moksha, or realizing the unity of brahman and atman, presupposes an initial alienation from this perception. In addition, the idea that following one’s dharma, which is to self-actualize through the practice of yoga according to one’s nature, reflects the stratified nature of the Hindu caste system. This contrasts sharply with the horizontal and undivided ecological role that Indigenous peoples inhabit: they, too, have a place in the cosmos, a part of something greater, but it does not resemble the distinct, bounded nature of one’s category according to dharma. One could read the requirement to achieve moksha as resembling the Platonic-Aristotelian conception of moral uprightness being in telological actualization of one’s inherent nature.

Finally, Buddhism denies the existence of permanence in a strong, ontological sense. For the Buddhist, constancy is an illusion and distortion wrought by attachment, which brings suffering. The ultimate attainment of Buddhist religion is total non-attachment and the accompanying understanding of the transient nature of all things. It is an ethic of repeated letting go and resignation that brings peace through understanding that one cannot control the external world and, in fact, this view is brought through attachment to past experiences that give the illusion of constancy. This expectation of constancy in the face of the reality of impermanence can only bring suffering.
