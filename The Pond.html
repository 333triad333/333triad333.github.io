I'm sitting at a table, surrounded by a group of the other residents, who are playing cards and trading gossip, same as any other day. Same as any other day, almost, what's unusual is that I sat down. Even then, to them, I’m part of the group. From my vantage, they are strangers to me. I think it is common for people who are effectively strangers to one another to consider each other friends.

"Them girls were fighting this morning and the staff didn't do nothing about it," Kathy says.

"I'm so sick of this place and the constant drama, it's just drama drama drama all the time and the staff doesn’t care. They just let it happen," Sonya adds.

"This is why I don't be hanging out here, I can't stand these people and their drama, I'm out all day."

This conversation happens a lot. This morning it was Nicole and this other girl and the staff that got into it. I don't know what happened, but I saw the aftermath. Police didn't show up this time, but the day is still young. It is apparent that Nicole has pride. Even people with nothing in particular to be proud of can have pride. Just pure assertion of self unadorned, bare ego. It feels good to exist and to think about yourself and for others to think about you. With Nicole it isn't disguised, she doesn't know that she needs to bother with hiding it and dressing it up in layers like a secret. The women at the table don't know that they're like Nicole, they can't see themselves. I know how I see this.

Ashamed, I want to act against the environment and against myself. I don't know how I can do this: what can I say? And, fatally, I realize I don't believe in them, and I feel hypocritical. But that's the difference: I see and they don't, I can feel like a hypocrite, I tell myself. That doesn't salve the wound, I hate the idea. I worry that if I speak, what I would say would be false.

Kathy intervenes.

"Want to play next game?"

"Sure," I say.

They finish up the round, shuffle, and distribute cards.

"You’re pretty quiet and I don't see you around here much. Where do you go all day?"

I recoil a bit. I don't want to answer the question. I feel a little angry, a little threatened. Anything I say is going to incur guilty feelings. I tell her the truth anyways.

"I change it up a lot, but I bounce between coffeeshops and restaurants. I just work all day."

"That's right, you’re a student, aren't you?"

"Yeah."

Kathy and then Sonya play their hands. I play mine. I come in second.

"Sick of this game," Sonya says.

"I'm getting sick of this place," Kathy rejoins.

They pack up the box of cards and chat for a little longer. I am invited on a trip to the store. I decline. Instead, I head to the pond, where I can look at snails.

It's a long bus ride out to the park, but I enjoy those, so it's no problem. I like to listen to music and look out the window at the scenery. Trees and buildings passing, the weight of past emotion attaches to the present and colors the world outside, living memory. I try to focus to deepen the feeling. Pure sensation. I'm not certain how I would show this experience to others, which troubles me. The most meaningful experiences in my life are deeply private, things that I've not attempted to articulate, that I don't know how to share. There's a chasm. I want to go out and bring it back, to share the places I've been with the world, and to find connection in that shared understanding. But I resist this too: maybe the resolution of tension, the unease, back and forth, should be resolved in the other direction. I can quiet the oscillation by detaching, a unilateral affirmation of my solitude. I can smother the nagging and the guilt and I can affirm a private life. I castigate myself for my cowardice: I'm afraid to be illegible. I split myself between guilty stealing of time, waiting to be caught and exposed in this asocial act. I am not sure that the impulse to sociality is true: it's reactive, defensive. Maybe I want to be alone. Every time I cheat myself of this is to dodge responsibility for my desire; maybe the guilt is not the harm done to others, but from abandoning myself.

Eventually I arrive at the park. It's a cold, crisp afternoon and the grass is browned from winter. I wander down the meandering dirt path to the pond. I start looking around for snails. Under leaves, in plants, piles of dirt mixed with shrubs. After a number of attempts I am successful. I find a tangle of snails clustered together. Slowly, I pry them apart, taking turns handling each one. At first, they curl up inside, afraid; but hold the shell for a few moments and they regain their courage and come out. Maybe they forget they’re being held, or are unable to register the situation accurately, maybe they can't form a model of the scenario. I'm not sure. I take immense pleasure in observing them.

When snails die, they recoil, inside, depleting to nothing backwards. I read a study that said that snails, loners, are unable to form long-term memories when isolated. Long-term memory, for a snail, spans the length of a day. Even for snails: memory requires history and history is history of individuals together. Snails, carrying their memory on their backs and living inside it. Snails withdraw into themselves to rest and for protection. This is also the circumstance of their death. What does a snail have to give to the world, to its cohabitants? A snail can only give itself, in love, the same as anyone else. The same as anyone else, this act perpetuates life, history, and individual memory.

When snails copulate, rigid calcium "love darts" are fired into the mate. I imagine it must be painful. If the mating fails, the snail withdraws. I wonder about the infertile snail, what that does. I imagine: a snail depleted of calcium, sterile and lacking in vitality, anemic. Snails are hermaphrodites, but cannot reproduce asexually.

I get a text from Kathy. She wants to know where I'm at. Responding feels overwhelming and I'm a bit peeved at a question that I perceive as intrusive. I don't respond.
