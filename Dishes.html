I need to do the dishes, she thinks. Next: I need to go to the grocery store. Dishes or grocery store, dishes and grocery store, can't have one without the other, right? But first? Which is harder? More important?

The dishes have been sitting for too long, much too long. She avoids the kitchen and, consequently, the bugs. The mold. I've let this go too long, she says, again I've let this go too long. Enters kitchen to do a threat assessment. Dishes were done last week: the immediacy of this week's presence and last week's passing hits like an attempt to comprehend a moving car. The indivisible moment and its extension. This week's mold is last week's clean sink. The grocery store is a machine co-extensive with the sink, one fills and the other empties, around and around, it seems to her. Does it really matter which state I begin in, she thinks. She thinks: surely there's some advantage to beginning in one state over the other, a lie meant to soothe her demand for a rational decision.

The wet food on her fingers, she tenses and flees to thought. Three more plates, what's next, the smell of the mold, I need to go to the store. The mold, thinking of cooking dinner. Three more plates, the wretched feel of the food. She pictures herself at the store, browsing the aisles, enslavement to what's next, the indefinite, the wet mold. She scrubs and loops again, the chicken, the store, the mold, the sponge, buy a new sponge, wet chicken for dinner. She's scrubbing the plate, she's preparing for dinner. So it goes, we do it again.
