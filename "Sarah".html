She wants to move in together even though we only met last week. Alarm bells ringing: bad idea for me, bad idea for her. I'm going to have to be the one to break the news, I can see the future. She thinks we'd get along for the same reason I'm feeling dread: I've failed to extend myself, claim my presence outwardly, act authentically in a social existence, once again. In my absence, a fantasy grows, I can be whatever, a blank screen for her desires and impositions. She's not conscious of this, they never are. The pressure is mounting; I can tell she's feeling rejected. She repeats her plans for the second time this hour. A big house with a yard, in the suburbs of Chicago, no, Florida, twenty minutes pass, she's staying here for a decade. Moving out of town with the new guy, she dumped him yesterday, can't stand the people around, going back to school, she's a nurse and will have a job any day now. She doesn't actually have her degree yet, though she's completed most of a criminal justice degree and is thinking of shifting to nursing. She wants to be a parole officer, a nurse, a parole officer. She's going to finish the criminal justice degree, got kicked out of school and is evasive about why, has something to do with grades, even though she's at the top of the class. She's going to be a nurse, loves old people, even though they're hard to deal with.

I'm dizzy. I tell her I need time to think. She tells me the plan, that I can trust her, I need time to think, ten minutes detailing the plan, she asks for my decision, I tell her I don't like living with people, she tells me the plan, I can trust her, she'll have a job any day now, she's going to get the kid back, it's better if he lives with her sister, big yard for the kid with the guy with the house. Have you made your decision? Anxious, I don't like living with other people, I say. That's not an acceptable answer, so I listen to the pitch. I ask for more time. She wants to close the sale.

We get some food, head back, she confronts me again,

I steel myself for the exhausting and tremendous monologue I'm about to give in an act of bad faith. I'm going to be dishonest, speak in a cadence I've learned from American television that makes people think I'm wise, respectable. I'm going to grandstand, look responsible, and in a way, that's what I'm doing, but I'm not being honest all the same; I can't inhabit the position I'm about to act out, I'm just defending myself from actually making emotional contact in a false display of bravery. People love it when I do this.

"Look, I think that moving in together is a bad idea. I'm older than you, I lived with roommates all throughout my twenties. It doesn't matter who you live with, having roommates sucks, and it's the quickest way to ruin a relationship with someone you like. I think the best thing to do is to be patient, work on getting a job, and get your own place. It's important to have as much control over your life as possible. You don't want to be dependent on another person to pay the rent. It also doesn't matter how well you get along with someone, I guarantee that after a few months you are going to find something you can't stand about living with the other person."

She looks impressed and the woman eavesdropping a table over does, too. After a moment, she speaks, praising my altruistic motives.

"I love that you have boundaries and are trying to keep this relationship healthy, and you know what, my job coach told me the same thing."

I'm intimidated, feeling raw. The authentic, simple answer invited an attempt at persuasion. Imperious, oversocialized people demand a certain affectation in your delivery. You have to put on the therapy voice.

A week later, she's gone: back to Minnesota with her ex. The immediacy fades into memory, chaos and fragmentation that haunts the present, ghostlike. It comes and goes; life feels like talking to her, always, each moment an analogue of her speech, then it's gone. Memory prefiguring experience, experience living through memory. It will be over before you know it.
