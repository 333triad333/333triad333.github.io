Parmenides

Parmenides' conception of the One influenced later thinking on the continuum. For Parmenides, reality was One and indivisible, and thus, the continuum was a single, unified whole with no parts. Through Zeno's defense of Parmenides against pluralistic metaphysics, the question of the whole and its parts entered philosophical discussion, having great impact on Aristotle's conception of continua.

Zeno

Zeno continued Parmenides' monism, and engenders a conceptual succession from the question of the many and the One to the existence or nonexistence of motion. Introducing his well-known paradoxes of the runner and Achilles and the tortoise, Zeno intends to defend Parmenides' monist metaphysics, and opens the way for Aristotle's rejection of Greek atomism and discussion of motion, the infinite and the continuum.

The paradox of the runner can be formulated as follows: in order for a runner to reach his goal, he must first arrive half-way at the goal, which can be infinitely subdivided into halves in this manner. He must, then, pass through an infinite number of points to reach his goal, which is impossible in a finite time.

This paradox, along with the identical problem presented in the paradox of Achilles and the tortoise, was meant to demonstrate the nonsensicality of a pluralist metaphysics. For if multiple things exist, then there must always be something between them, and this division can continue on indefinitely. Thus we reach a contradiction: if pluralism were true, we would have a whole made up of parts with no magnitude, and one would have to conclude that nothing exists. This consideration preoccupied modern mathematicians concerned with density and the composition of the continuum.

Aristotle

In Aristotle's Physics VI, the continuous is defined as a thing whose extremities are 'together', meaning located in the same place. Indivisibles cannot have extremities, for then they would be able to be divided into parts, meaning they would not be indivisibles. Because things that are continuous are united in their extremities as one, continuous things cannot be divided into indivisibles and, in fact, are infinitely divisible.

Aristotle continues on to show that motion is contuinuous, ie, infinitely divisible. Assuming that motion and, thus, time, can be divided into indivisibles, a motion AB over a distance YZ takes up an indivisible, discrete unit of time; but considering a faster motion CD over the same distance YZ, we find that CD must divide the motion AB into smaller units, contradicting the supposition that motion and, consequently, time, can be reduced to indivisibles. This process can be repeated indefinitely, suggesting that motion and, thus, time, is continuous.

Next, Aristotle argues that the present is indivisible. First, the present is the boundary between the past and the future, and the boundary belongs to both past and future. If the present was not indivisible, then the present would be divisible into parts, and would thus contain both parts of the past and future, making it not present. Thus, the present is indivisible. Contunuing, Aristotle claims that there is no motion in the present: for if there was, the present would be divisible, as per the appeal to the continuity of motion.

A thing that is changing has a definite end of change, but cannot be said to have begun changing at any point. This is because if, on the contrary, there was a beginning of change, then the moment immediately preceding it would be simultaneous with that moment, which is impossible because of the contunity of time.

Aristotle writes:

"But there are two senses of the expression 'the primary when in whcih something has changed.' One the one hand it may mean the primary when containing the completion of the process of change-- the moment when it is correct to say 'it has changed': on the other hand it may mean the primary when containing the beginning of the process of change. Now the primary when that has reference to the end of the change is something really existent: for a change may really be completed, and there is such a thing as an end of change, which we have in fact shown to be indivisible because it is a limit. But that which has reference to the beginning is not existent at all: for there is no such thing as a beginning of a process of change, and the time occupied by the change does not contain any primary in which the change began."

Aristotle is, conceptually, anticipating Dedekind here: dividing the rationals into two cuts, the lower of which being unbounded above, expresses the idea of a change with no beginning, tending to a limit: a real number, which is an Aristotelian indivisible, insofar as it expresses its status as a number. The Aristotelian end of change becomes Dedekind's limit point.

Now addressing Zeno's paradox of motion directly, Aristotle shows that it is impossible for a finite change to occur in an infinite amount of time, or vice versa. This seems to prefigure, conceptually, Cantor's idea of using a bijection to compare the sizes of infinite sets: that infinite time cannot be divided by finite time, ie, there is an incommensurable difference in size between the two. A finite time is made up of finite parts, underscoring the qualitative difference between the finite and infinite. As the argument goes, an infinite magnitude cannot be covered by a finite magnitude, for at each division, the finite magnitude would again be able to be divided, falling short of the infinite magnitude. This "zooming in" mirrors the process of revealing the density, or infinite divisibility, of the real line, the image evoked by Cantor's formal argument that finite or countably infinite sets cannot be put into one-to-one correspondence with uncountably infinite sets.

For Aristotle, we can only speak of the motion of a part as a consequence of the motion of the whole. The whole is prior to the part, and motion of a subordinate indivisible part is only moving by virtue of the movement of the whole.

Diodorus Cronus and Greek Atomism

Diodorus Cronus followed the Greek atomists, postulating the existence of indivisibles possessing physical extension. This view suggests discontinuous motion: a partless body cannot be moving in the place where it is, as the space it occupies is filled, but it likewise cannot be said to be moving where it is not, meaning that, following Aristotle's objection to the atomists, that motion occurs in discontinuous "jerks" of atomic length. Diodorus seems to have been committed to the existence of atomic times, which, per Aristotle, atomic motion would seem to require.
